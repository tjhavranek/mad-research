# MAD-research final memo

## Verdict
The central negative claim survives: the manuscript gives credible evidence that broad, unconditional exercise-cognition claims are weakened by publication bias and extreme heterogeneity. Identification credibility is weaker for the precise adjusted effects and positive subgroup claims because comparator type, duplicated primary-study origins, and residual intervention categories are not sufficiently modeled. Statistical validity is ambitious but fragile where publication-bias estimates depend on reconstructed standard errors and where RoBMA variants disagree. Robustness coverage is better than in a conventional meta-analysis, but not broad enough to justify “ensure the robustness” language. Numerical/citation accuracy is mostly adequate, with one important count/provenance reconciliation problem. The severity-weighted issue count points to major revision: the field-corrective message can remain, but the adjusted magnitudes, subgroup recommendations, and contribution framing need tightening.

## Top surviving criticisms (5-7, by severity)

1. Unit-of-analysis dependence is unresolved; Severity: Severe; Evidence: “we were unable to track the origin of all extracted study-level estimates. While it is possible some estimates were included multiple times, the degree of overlap is likely less than 1% based on the assessment by Singh et al. (2025)” (p.20, Limitations); Why it matters: The analysis is described as study-level, but the quoted limitation means some rows may not be independent primary-study information. [EXTERNAL] Unmodeled positive dependence can overstate effective sample size, narrow uncertainty, and inflate Bayes-factor evidence. The borrowed “less than 1%” statement does not demonstrate row-level duplication in this retained dataset, especially in small subgroups. Supporting audits: X Round 1 and Round 2; Y Round 1.

2. Standard-error reconstruction is load-bearing and insufficiently stress-tested; Severity: Severe; Evidence: “All analyses were performed on standardized mean differences (either Cohen’s d or Hedge’s g) as reported in the original meta-analysis. For 422 studies, both effect sizes and standard errors were directly available. For 1,828 studies, standard errors were computed using a normal approximation based on the confidence interval width. For 62 studies, standard errors were computed from the effect size and total sample size under the assumption of equal group sizes” (p.24, Statistical analysis); Why it matters: The main funnel, z-curve, PET-PEESE, and selection-model claims depend directly on SMD/SE pairs. The manuscript also says “215 meta-analyses containing 2,239 effect size estimates were retained” after removal (p.23), while the SE-source categories sum to the earlier 2,312 estimates, so readers cannot tell whether SE provenance describes the analyzed sample. Supporting audits: X Round 1 and Round 2; Y Round 1 and Round 2.

3. The causal estimand is blurred by comparator heterogeneity; Severity: Serious; Evidence: “reviews were eligible if ≥ 75% of the included RCTs compared exercise to no intervention, waitlist, usual care, nothing, a sham intervention, an equal attention non-exercise intervention arm or a lower/lesser exercise intervention” (p.22, Methods); Why it matters: Passive controls, attention controls, sham controls, and lower-dose exercise controls answer different questions. The paper itself notes that prior effects “attenuated after accounting for key moderators (active control groups and baseline differences; SMD = 0.13)” (p.3), yet comparator type is not listed among the moderators used before subgrouping. Supporting audits: X Round 1.

4. Publication-bias correction is treated more confidently than the diagnostics allow; Severity: Serious; Evidence: “RoBMA_PSMA finds strong evidence for publication bias operating via inflated results in small studies... RoBMA_WF finds extreme evidence against publication bias operating via selection for significance or direction of the results” (p.9, Memory), and “questionable research practices... can result in over- or under-adjusted pooled effect size estimates” (p.20, Limitations); Why it matters: The headline adjusted estimates are useful sensitivity results, not a single recovered truth. This matters most for memory and executive function, where alternative bias ensembles move the conclusion materially. The introduction’s claim that the methods “ensure the robustness of our results” (p.4) is too strong. Supporting audits: X Round 1; Y Round 2; Z Round 1 and Round 2.

5. Exploratory-looking subgroup signals are promoted into recommendation language; Severity: Serious; Evidence: “The moderator analysis did not show evidence of moderation by any of our moderators... Therefore, we performed subgroup analyses by population type, population age, and exercise category” (p.7, General cognition), while the abstract says “the evidence supports targeted, population- and intervention-specific recommendations” (p.2, Abstract); Why it matters: Subgroup analysis after weak omnibus moderation can be useful for hypothesis generation, but it should not be framed as a recommendation basis without multiplicity control, cross-cell shrinkage, or explicit decision thresholds. [EXTERNAL] Recommendation claims normally require a decision context and replication standard beyond exploratory subgroup contrasts. Supporting audits: X Round 1; Z Round 1 and Round 2.

6. The residual “other” exercise category cannot carry targeted intervention claims; Severity: Moderate; Evidence: exercise types include “other (all estimates not matching any of the previous categories, 1278 estimates)” (p.24, Data extraction), while the introduction lists “other physical activity for executive function in non-healthy children and adolescents” as “promising evidence for the benefit” (p.4, Introduction); Why it matters: A catch-all category may be necessary for coding, but it is not an interpretable exercise intervention. Treating it as part of the map of “under what conditions” exercise helps weakens reproducibility and practical meaning. Supporting audits: Y Round 1; Z Round 2.

7. The contribution is described as more comprehensive than the extractable evidence supports; Severity: Moderate; Evidence: “This study advances the exercise–cognition literature by providing a comprehensive, study-level perspective” (p.4, Introduction), but “We successfully extracted 2,239 study-level estimates and characteristics from 215 out of 286 (74.4%) individual meta-analyses” and the rest “could not be obtained due to missing study-information tables, forest plots, or supplementary information” (p.4-p.5, Results); Why it matters: The dataset is large and valuable, but it is conditional on extractability and on Singh et al.’s prior search and inclusion pipeline. The manuscript should call this a study-level re-analysis of extractable evidence rather than a fully comprehensive field map. Supporting audits: Z Round 1 and Round 2; Y Round 1.

## Minority report
A grounded minority objection should be preserved: the manuscript may understate the positive general-cognition result in its broad cautionary phrasing. The relevant passage is strong: “the notably reduced publication bias-adjusted overall estimate (SMD = 0.227 [0.116, 0.330])” is “supported by extreme evidence for the presence of the effect (Bayes factor in favor of effect, BF₁₀ = 400.6)” (p.7, General cognition). A reader could reasonably ask for more nuance before the statement that “strong recommendations that exercise improves cognition are not warranted” (p.17, Discussion). This minority objection does not defeat the main conclusion because the same p.7 passage says the result is “of little value due to the extreme between-study heterogeneity,” but it does justify more construct-specific wording.

## Points rejected under scrutiny

| Claim | Reason |
|---|---|
| The 217/2,312 versus 215/2,239 counts are a direct contradiction. | Rejected as a standalone contradiction. The manuscript says “217 out of the 286 meta-analyses (with 2,312 estimates)” were available, then after re-examination “2 meta-analyses were completely removed” and “215 meta-analyses containing 2,239 effect size estimates were retained” (p.23). The surviving issue is the later SE-provenance counts, not this pre/post-cleaning sequence. |
| Publication-bias adjustment is presented everywhere as causal truth. | Overstated. The manuscript explicitly warns that QRPs “can result in over- or under-adjusted pooled effect size estimates” (p.20) and says pooled effects “should be interpreted with extreme caution” (p.18). The surviving criticism is narrower: abstract and contribution language overstate robustness. |
| The closest-model extraction validation invalidates the dataset. | Rejected. The text says cases with larger discrepancies were “re-examined” and two were removed (p.23). More disclosure would help, but the procedure is not inherently invalid. |
| The manuscript attacks exercise guidance broadly. | Rejected. The paper explicitly states, “Our findings should not dissuade individuals from exercising, as there are many other benefits associated with regular exercise” (p.20). |
| All subgroup findings should be discarded. | Rejected. The issue is not that subgroup analyses are useless; it is that “promising evidence” (p.18) should not become “targeted... recommendations” (p.2) without additional safeguards. |

## Conventional-referee assumption check
A conventional referee might assume that “study-level” automatically means independent primary trials; the manuscript’s own origin-tracking limitation prevents that assumption. A conventional referee might also accept Bayesian model averaging as blanket robustness, but the manuscript’s QRP caveat and RoBMA disagreement show that the bias-adjusted estimates are model-specific sensitivity results. The large sample size should not obscure the fact that most SEs are reconstructed and one large exercise bin is residual. At the same time, a conventional skeptical referee should not overcorrect: the negative, heterogeneity-centered message is well supported by the paper’s own prediction-interval framing.

## Action list
1. Define target estimands by outcome, population, intervention type, and comparator type; add comparator type as a moderator or sensitivity analysis.
2. Create a row-level provenance table linking extracted estimates to primary RCTs where possible; deduplicate or cluster by primary study and report effective sample sizes.
3. Reconcile all pre-removal and post-removal counts, including SE-source counts by retained outcome dataset.
4. Add SE-provenance sensitivity checks: direct-SE subset, CI-derived subset, equal-group assumption stress test, and harmonized Cohen’s d/Hedges’ g analysis.
5. Present RoBMA_PSMA and RoBMA_WF as alternative sensitivity estimates where they disagree, and soften “ensure robustness” language.
6. Reanalyze subgroup evidence with hierarchical shrinkage or explicit multiplicity accounting; state the number of tested cells and downgrade recommendation language to hypothesis-generating where appropriate.
7. Break down the “other” exercise category or remove targeted claims based on it; narrow “comprehensive” to “extractable study-level re-analysis.”
