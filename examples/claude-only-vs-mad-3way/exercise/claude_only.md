# MAD-research final memo (Claude-only arm)

## Verdict

This is a methodologically serious, unusually transparent study-level re-analysis of the Singh et al. (2025) exercise-cognition corpus, and its central qualitative thesis — that publication-bias-adjusted pooled effects are far smaller than headline claims and that "extreme between-study heterogeneity" (τ = 0.647 [0.596, 0.704] for general cognition, p.7) renders the pooled means nearly uninformative — survives scrutiny on the evidence the paper itself presents. The move from meta-analysis-level summaries to study-level effect sizes is the correct fix for the identification limitation it targets ("The extent of between-study heterogeneity and publication bias cannot be evaluated when combining meta-analytic summary estimates," p.18), and reporting heterogeneity as τ and prediction intervals rather than I² is exemplary practice. The paper is also candid about its own soft spots (propagated extraction errors, p.19; possible QRP misspecification of the bias model, p.20; <1% borrowed overlap figure, p.20). The weaknesses are concentrated not in the conclusion's direction but in three places: an estimand that averages over five qualitatively different comparators that are never coded; affirmative subgroup recommendations promoted to the abstract after a null omnibus moderation test with no multiplicity discipline; and an abstract that reports three precise positive point estimates with credible intervals while omitting the prediction intervals the body calls decision-relevant. None of these is fatal, but several are publication-blocking in their current framing and the named "promising" subgroups should be demoted to exploratory.

## Top surviving criticisms (5-7, by severity)

### 1. The estimand averages over five qualitatively different comparators that are never coded or modeled, so part of the load-bearing heterogeneity is mechanical contrast-mixing
- **Severity:** High
- **Evidence:** Inclusion required only that "≥ 75% of the included RCTs compared exercise to no intervention, waitlist, usual care, nothing, a sham intervention, an equal attention non-exercise intervention arm or a lower/lesser exercise intervention" (p.22). The extracted moderators are "population type... population age... intervention duration... exercise intensity... and exercise types" (p.23) — comparator type is absent. The paper's own intro credits active-control adjustment with halving the prior effect: Ciria et al.'s benefit "attenuated after accounting for key moderators (active control groups and baseline differences; SMD = 0.13)" (p.3).
- **Why it matters:** An SMD against a waitlist/no-intervention control and an SMD against an "equal attention non-exercise intervention" or a "lower/lesser exercise intervention" estimate different causal contrasts (the latter net out expectancy/attention and can be near-zero by construction). Averaging them yields a composite with no single policy interpretation, and their dispersion inflates τ independently of any true variation in the exercise effect. Because the entire interpretive thesis — pooled means uninformative, PIs spanning harm and benefit (PI[−1.052, 1.500], p.7) — turns on the magnitude of τ, attributing that τ to "between-study heterogeneity" in the effect (p.4, p.18) before removing comparator heterogeneity is an unvalidated identifying assumption. The same contamination flows into every subgroup cell, which are defined by population×exercise but never by comparator. The strongest defense (control type is unextractable because the source meta-analyses pooled across controls) is real but partial: the authors hand-coded exercise type, population, age, intensity, and duration per estimate (p.23), so control type is no less extractable in principle, and they themselves name active-control adjustment as the mechanism that halved the prior effect (p.3).

### 2. Affirmative subgroup recommendations are promoted to the abstract after a null omnibus moderation test, with no multiplicity rule and several single-meta-analysis cells
- **Severity:** High
- **Evidence:** "The moderator analysis did not show evidence of moderation by any of our moderators (population type, population age, exercise intensity, exercise category, or intervention duration)... Therefore, we performed subgroup analyses" (p.7). The abstract then states "Subgroup analyses identify specific population-intervention combinations with more consistent benefits" (p.2), and the intro lists "promising evidence for the benefit of: (i) resistance training in healthy older adults, and dance and exergaming for general cognition in non-healthy older adults..." (p.4). The thinnest highlighted cell, exergaming in non-healthy older adults, is "based on only 9 studies from a single meta-analysis" (p.9; Figure 2, p.8, shows k = 9, m = 1); resistance training in healthy older adults is m = 5 but resistance in healthy adults is k = 24, m = 1 (Figure 2).
- **Why it matters:** A null model-averaged moderation test removes the data-based license to partition by those very factors; reading the surviving Bayes factors across ~15 population×exercise cells per outcome as evidence for "targeted recommendations" is selection-on-the-outcome / garden-of-forking-paths. With m = 1 the between-meta-analysis variance is unidentified, so "consistency" is indistinguishable from a single review's idiosyncrasies (including its own publication bias). The paper states no minimum-m, no multiplicity correction, and no replication-across-meta-analyses rule. The hedge "the substantial heterogeneity precludes any general recommendations" (p.20) is genuine but does not cover naming m = 1 cells as showing "more consistent benefits" in the abstract, where the exploratory status is dropped exactly where guideline writers will read.

### 3. The bias correction is the load-bearing identifying step, yet the two co-equal RoBMA models flip the direction of evidence for the headline outcomes and the paper runs no prior/specification sensitivity analysis
- **Severity:** High
- **Evidence:** For executive function, "RoBMA_WF provides a publication bias-adjusted estimate to approximately 50% of the original estimate (SMD = 0.116 [0.000, 0.184]) supported by strong evidence for the presence of the effect (BF₁₀ = 15.9). However, RoBMA_PSMA shrinks the publication bias-adjusted overall estimate towards zero (SMD = 0.012 [0.000, 0.147]) with moderate evidence against the presence of the effect (BF₁₀ = 1/6.41)" (p.12). For memory the bias direction itself reverses: "RoBMA_PSMA finds strong evidence for publication bias operating via inflated results in small studies (BF_bias = 19.4...), RoBMA_WF finds extreme evidence against publication bias... (BF_bias = 1/45.5)" (p.9). Priors are fixed at defaults: "We specified default prior distributions... τ ∼ Inv-Gamma(1, 0.15)... The null hypotheses were specified via spikes at zero" (p.27); 50/50 prior model-probability split (p.27-28). The paper concedes the mechanism may be misspecified: "the observed distribution of test statistics indicated a higher skew near the significance criterion than expected under publication bias alone. This may indicate questionable research practices (QRPs...), which can result in over- or under-adjusted pooled effect size estimates" (p.20).
- **Why it matters:** The presence-vs-absence-of-effect conclusion — the paper's headline claim that effects "diminish to zero for memory and executive function" (p.17) — inverts across two configurations of the same framework, so it is not robust, and its dependence on the heterogeneity prior, the spike-at-zero nulls, and the model-probability split is an unexamined degree of freedom. Bayesian model averaging legitimately retains model uncertainty, which defends *reporting* both, but BMA cannot average away a data-generating mechanism (QRPs) that none of the candidate models represents, and a no-sensitivity-analysis omission leaves the "shrunk to zero" claims model-contingent. This is robustness-of-identification, not a presentational quibble.

### 4. The cross-construct contrast that anchors the narrative rests on an outcome the authors concede confounds construct with measurement and selective reporting
- **Severity:** Medium
- **Evidence:** General cognition is the sole headline outcome with a positive lower bound (SMD = 0.227 [0.116, 0.330], p.7; abstract p.2), versus memory 0.027 and executive function 0.012 at/near zero. The authors state: "general cognition... is treated as a distinct construct, typically assessed using composite screening measures (e.g., MMSE, MoCA) in older or clinical samples. These measures often yield larger effects than domain-specific tests, reflecting both differences in measurement and selective reporting" (p.18).
- **Why it matters:** The abstract's three-way contrast implies a differential biological response across cognitive domains, but the authors' own explanation attributes the general-cognition elevation partly to coarse screening instruments administered to impaired populations and to selective reporting. SMD is scale-free but that does not remove differential ceiling/floor sensitivity across instruments. Disclosing the confound in the Discussion does not neutralize a comparative claim foregrounded in the abstract and policy framing; instrument type (screening composite vs domain test) is never entered as a moderator within general cognition.

### 5. Cross-meta-analysis trial overlap is asserted, not measured, and the "<1%" figure is borrowed at the wrong accounting unit
- **Severity:** Medium
- **Evidence:** "While it is possible some estimates were included multiple times, the degree of overlap is likely less than 1% based on the assessment by Singh et al. (2025)" (p.20). The three-level model handles estimates nested in meta-analyses via "multilevel versions when dealing with estimates from different meta-analyses" (p.27), and the cited dependent-effect-size method (Bartoš, Maier & Wagenmakers 2025; verified in references p.29) models nesting *within* meta-analyses.
- **Why it matters:** In an umbrella-review corpus drawn from 286 meta-analyses of overlapping RCT pools, the same primary trial routinely enters multiple meta-analyses — a distinct dependence structure from within-meta-analysis nesting, and the cited method does not cover it. If shared RCTs are treated as independent draws, effective sample size is overstated and credible intervals / Bayes factors are too narrow (BF₁₀ = 400.6, p.7; BF_bias > 1.00 × 10⁶, p.7). The "<1%" is imported from Singh's different accounting and never recomputed at the estimate level on the 2,239 retained estimates. Fix: cross-reference primary-study identifiers and add a primary-study (crossed) random effect.

### 6. Novelty is overstated relative to Ciria et al. (2023), whose adjusted estimate and qualitative conclusion this paper reproduces
- **Severity:** Medium
- **Evidence:** The contribution is framed as a "comprehensive, study-level perspective that earlier meta-meta-analyses and umbrella reviews could not offer" (p.4). But the Discussion concedes the central result reproduces a two-year-old finding: the adjusted estimates "are shrunk to a similar degree as the pooled adjusted estimate (SMD = 0.05 [−0.09, 0.14]) reported by Ciria et al. (2023)" (p.17), and the intro already records that Ciria et al. found benefits that "became negligible after publication-bias adjustment (SMD = 0.05)" and flagged "underpowered designs, salami slicing, regression to the mean, placebo-like effects, selective reporting, and publication bias" (p.3).
- **Why it matters:** The qualitative thesis (pooled effects shrink to near-zero; heterogeneity is extreme; generalized-enhancement claims are premature) is not new — it is Ciria et al.'s conclusion on overlapping trials. The genuine increment is bounded and methodological (study-level rather than meta-analysis-level data, larger trial base, formal RoBMA with prediction intervals), which is real and worthwhile; the abstract's framing implies a substantive overturning the field's most relevant predecessor had already established. Reposition as confirming-and-extending Ciria et al. at the study level.

## Minority report

Preserved from Audit Z's designated minority objection, which I judge grounded: **the panel should not treat this paper's headline conclusion as a novel substantive contribution.** By the authors' own statement (p.17) the bias-adjusted estimates are "shrunk to a similar degree" as Ciria et al. (2023)'s SMD = 0.05, and Ciria et al. had already enumerated the same battery of problems (p.3) two years earlier. The increment here is methodological refinement at the study level, not a new finding — and a referee panel that lets the abstract's "premature / targeted recommendations" framing stand unqualified would be endorsing an overclaim of originality that the paper's own Discussion contradicts. I dissent from any consensus that scores the contribution as field-changing rather than confirmatory-plus-method.

## Conventional-referee assumption check

A conventional referee would likely accept four premises this audit declines to grant. (1) *That presenting RoBMA_WF and RoBMA_PSMA side by side is sufficient robustness.* It is not: the two flip the direction of evidence for memory and executive function (p.9, p.12) and neither varies the priors, so the "shrunk to zero" headline is model-contingent in a way a reader cannot bound. (2) *That a study-level re-analysis inherits the credibility of its source corpus.* It inherits the source's search, screening, and inclusion ("relies on its search, screening, and inclusion procedures," p.21), so any selection or construct error in Singh propagates; the new bias machinery cannot remove corpus-level selection. (3) *That disclosing a limitation in prose neutralizes it.* The paper discloses the comparator/measurement confounds and the m = 1 caveat, but disclosure is not conditioning — the comparative and subgroup claims still propagate into the abstract unhedged. (4) *That a null omnibus moderation test followed by stratified "promising" subgroups is normal exploratory practice.* Stratified looks can be informative when heterogeneity differs across levels (the paper's stated rationale, p.7), but without a pre-committed minimum-m or multiplicity rule the affirmative cells are indistinguishable from forking-paths artifacts. On the positive side, a conventional referee's instinct that the paper is transparent and reproducible is correct: priors, model counts (4×9 = 36; 4×7 = 28, p.26), software version (RoBMA 3.6.0, p.27), extraction validation (p.23), and an OSF link (p.20) are all present.

## Action list

1. **Code comparator type per estimate** (passive vs active/attention vs dose-comparison) and add it as a meta-regression moderator and/or stratifier; at minimum report a sensitivity analysis restricted to passive controls so that τ is not conflating contrast-definition variance with true effect heterogeneity (addresses #1).
2. **Demote the named subgroups to exploratory** in the abstract and intro; state the number of cells examined per outcome, apply a multiplicity-aware framing, and set a minimum m (e.g., m ≥ 3) before any cell is described as showing "more consistent benefits." Remove or explicitly flag the m = 1 highlights (addresses #2).
3. **Run and report a prior/specification sensitivity analysis** for the headline outcomes (vary τ ∼ Inv-Gamma hyperparameters, the spike-at-zero nulls, and the model-probability split), and pre-justify one primary bias model with a QRP-robust check, since RoBMA_WF and RoBMA_PSMA invert the direction of evidence (addresses #3).
4. **Add the prediction interval to the abstract** alongside each SMD (e.g., "SMD = 0.227, but PI −1.05 to 1.50, spanning harm and benefit") so the abstract carries the heterogeneity-forward message the body designates as decision-relevant.
5. **Report instrument type as a moderator within general cognition** (screening composite vs domain test) and avoid cross-construct comparative claims unless adjusted for measurement and population mix (addresses #4).
6. **Quantify estimate-level trial overlap** by cross-referencing primary-study identifiers across the 215 meta-analyses, and either deduplicate or add a primary-study (crossed) random effect; replace the borrowed "<1%" with a figure computed on the 2,239 retained estimates (addresses #5).
7. **Reposition the contribution** explicitly as confirming-and-extending Ciria et al. (2023) at the study level with formal bias modeling, stating in the abstract what is incremental over Ciria et al. specifically, not only over Singh et al. (addresses #6).
8. **Reconcile the bookkeeping** before publication: report SE-provenance and absolute-difference buckets on the final 215-meta-analysis / 2,239-estimate dataset, correct "74.4%" to 75.2%, harmonize the 213-vs-215 per-outcome counts, and fix the four reference-list defects (Mathur title; Maier bare DOI; Gronau and Hoeting %5C%2F links).
