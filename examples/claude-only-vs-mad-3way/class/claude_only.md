# MAD-research final memo (Claude-only arm)

## Verdict

This is a careful, unusually self-critical meta-analysis (Opatrny, Havranek, Irsova & Scasny, *Journal of Labor Economics*, forthcoming) whose **qualitative** finding — little publication bias in the class-size literature — is genuinely well-triangulated across linear, nonlinear, Bayesian, and frequentist machinery, and survives even the orthogonality-free estimators (MAIVE, Elliott et al.). Its **quantitative** headline — that the implied class-size effect is "negligible" — is materially weaker than presented. The bottom-line number (-0.21) is a conditional fitted value at an analyst-chosen "best-practice" covariate bundle whose most influential inputs (STAR, cross-sectional data) mechanically pull toward zero, and its confidence interval [-1.38, 0.97] spans effects the paper's own benchmark calls non-trivial. The paper's own specification test (Table 4) rejects the zero-correlation assumption underlying the Egger/selection corrections, which it then attributes to heterogeneity without a test. The single randomized design (STAR) is reasoned away as an "anomaly" by extrapolation across lower-credibility observational studies, inverting the usual evidence hierarchy. None of this overturns the central contribution; the fixes are largely rhetorical and tabular, but the abstract and policy verdict outrun what is identified.

## Top surviving criticisms (by severity)

### 1. The headline bias correction rests on an orthogonality assumption the authors' own diagnostic rejects — then assumed away as "heterogeneity"
- **Severity: High**
- **Evidence:** "If all assumptions of the selection model hold, estimates and standard errors, re-weighted by the inverse publication probability computed by the selection model, should be uncorrelated. Table 4 shows the results for various subsets of data: we always find a substantial correlation. The finding indicates that the zero correlation assumption is tenuous" (p.19). Table 4 reports correlations of 0.555 (all), 0.835 (RDD), 0.609 (IV), 0.487 (FE), 0.695 (preferred) (p.18).
- **Why it matters:** The Egger-type regressions (Table 3 Panel A constants, p.16) and the Andrews & Kasy selection model share the premise that, absent selection, the estimate-SE relationship is zero. The Kranz & Putz test in Table 4 is precisely that premise's check, and it rejects across every subset. The paper attributes the residual to heterogeneity (p.19) rather than to the p-hacking/Taylor's-law mechanism it itself invokes (p.13-15), but that attribution is an untested identifying assumption, not a finding. The escape hatch — MAIVE and Elliott et al. "do not rely on the orthogonality assumption" (p.19) — is asserted to be "broadly in line" (p.19) but the MAIVE corrected mean is never tabulated as such in the visible paper (the closest is the IV-column constant -0.345 in Table 3, p.16, which the text does not equate to MAIVE). So the reader cannot confirm the corrected *magnitude* survives once the failed assumption is dropped; only the *direction* of "little bias" clearly survives.

### 2. The "negligible effect" headline converts a wide two-sided interval into a demonstrated null
- **Severity: High**
- **Evidence:** Abstract: "The implied class size effect is negligible for all identification approaches except Tennessee's Student/Teacher Achievement Ratio project and for all contexts except classes of fewer than 15 students" (p.1). Table 8: "Overall corrected mean -0.21 [-1.38, 0.97]" (p.28). The body concedes the estimate is "virtually zero, albeit with a wide confidence interval" (p.27).
- **Why it matters:** The CI half-width (~1.18) exceeds the point estimate in absolute value, and the upper bound (0.97) sits essentially at the paper's own economic-significance boundary of |1| (effects "below 1 in absolute value are relatively small," p.8). "Failure to reject zero" and "estimated to be zero" are different inferential states with opposite policy implications; the paper's own "challenges the literature has faced in identifying the effect" (p.30) implies low power, which should widen the authors' uncertainty about the null rather than license "negligible." The hedge present in the body (p.27) is dropped from the abstract and conclusion.

### 3. The estimand silently shifts to a conditional prediction at an analyst-chosen "best-practice" point, and the conditioning drives the near-zero result
- **Severity: High**
- **Evidence:** "The implied effects are computed as fitted values from the Bayesian model averaging exercise. ... the overall mean, in the bottom line, is conditional on the following choices: no publication bias (which means we plug in zero for the standard error), preference for panel data (cross-sectional data = 0), preference for experimental or quasi-experimental estimates (OLS = 0) ... we set the variable Preferred to 1 and the variable Discounted to 0. Other variables are set to their sample means" (p.27). The conditioning variables include the most influential moderators: "STAR experiment -1.52 ... 1.00" and "Cross-sectional data -1.04 ... 1.00" (Table 7, p.25).
- **Why it matters:** -0.21 is the value of a regression surface at a normatively chosen covariate bundle, not a population-average treatment effect. Because STAR and cross-section are PIP=1.00 movers, plugging in "no STAR, panel data" mechanically pushes the prediction toward zero. Readers will read -0.21 as "the effect of class size"; it is "the effect in a study with the authors' preferred feature bundle." The paper never argues this bundle is the policy-relevant counterfactual. (Devil's advocate, credited: the recipe is fully disclosed, and down-weighting OLS/cross-sections is defensible — but transparency about a recipe is not identification of a target parameter.)

### 4. STAR — the only randomized design — is dismissed by extrapolation, inverting the evidence hierarchy
- **Severity: High**
- **Evidence:** "We show that these results are a robust feature of the STAR experiment data, not an artifact due to publication bias or methodological approaches... But we also show that the STAR experiment fails to replicate... It is possible that the quasi-experimental studies share a problem that collectively prevents them from identifying the true effect. We find it more likely that the STAR experiment was an anomaly" (p.30). STAR is a tight, large effect: "STAR experiment 56 -1.99 [-2.19, -1.78]" unweighted (Table 2, p.12), vs "Quasi-experimental 2,006 -0.38" (p.12); STAR PIP 1.00, posterior mean -1.52 (Table 7, p.25). The paper itself states OLS "is unlikely to succeed in recovering the causal estimate" (p.5).
- **Why it matters:** A meta-analytic mean is a coherent estimand only if its inputs identify the same parameter. The paper concedes STAR survives every bias correction yet reclassifies it as the outlier on a majority-vote-plus-single-replication basis, never modeling why dozens of distinct quasi-experimental designs would all attenuate. The more parsimonious reading — that the designs estimate genuinely different, heterogeneous estimands (a LATE among young children in very small classes for STAR vs. associations elsewhere) — is never tested. The Lang (2025) t-stat argument ("a t-statistic of about 5.48 ... only one out of twenty preferred estimates for the STAR experiment ... exceeds (narrowly) the 5.48 t-statistic threshold," p.30) is clever and applied honestly, but asymmetrically: the same power bar is not turned on the near-zero quasi-experimental pool, whose Table 8 CIs (RDD [-1.36, 1.00], IV [-1.59, 0.75]) are equally wide. It argues STAR is underpowered to *confirm* an effect, not that the effect is *absent*.

### 5. Table 4 confidence intervals are internally impossible in the rows that carry the key diagnostic
- **Severity: Medium**
- **Evidence:** Table 4 (p.18): "All estimates 0.555 [0.413, 0.537]" (point 0.555 > upper bound 0.537); "Regression discontinuity 0.835 [0.606, 0.816]" (0.835 > 0.816); "Instrumental variable 0.609 [0.369, 0.599]" (0.609 > 0.599). A 95% CI must bracket its own point estimate. The other three rows are internally consistent (FE 0.487 [0.383, 0.59]; Endogeneity ignored 0.526 [0.37, 0.63]; Preferred 0.695 [0.48, 0.72]).
- **Why it matters:** Both other audit streams lean on Table 4 as the paper's own evidence that orthogonality "is tenuous," and the *direction* survives, but the printed upper bounds in three of six rows are below their point estimates — most plausibly transposed digits (e.g., 0.537 for 0.573). The precise intervals quoted elsewhere from this table are therefore not trustworthy as printed, and the bootstrap replication count/seed for these correlations is undocumented. This is a table-integrity/reproducibility defect, not a substantive challenge to the conclusion.

### 6. The abstract's carved-out exceptions (small classes; disadvantaged students) rest on intervals that include zero
- **Severity: Medium**
- **Evidence:** Abstract carves out "classes of fewer than 15 students" (p.1); Table 8 reports "Class size = 15 -0.99 [-2.18, 0.21]" — CI crosses zero (p.28); the body hedges only "the implied effect reaches -1" (p.27). The introduction also states "Even disadvantaged students seem to benefit little from class size reductions" (p.2), yet Table 2 reports "Disadvantaged students 166 -0.18 [-0.53, 0.18]" (CI crosses zero, n=166) and Table 8 reports "Disadvantaged students -0.19 [-1.42, 1.03]" (p.28).
- **Why it matters:** The parallel "except" construction presents the small-class effect as an established exception on par with STAR (whose CI [-3.04, 0.04] also barely includes zero), yet by the paper's own intervals neither is distinguishable from zero. The disadvantaged-student claim is the equity clincher most likely to be quoted by policymakers, asserted as fact for a small, noisy subgroup whose interval spans from a meaningful negative effect to a meaningful positive one.

### 7. Within-study dependence among 2,434 estimates reaches the SEs but not the bottom-line estimand
- **Severity: Medium**
- **Evidence:** "We gather 2,819 estimates ... in 66 primary studies; 2,434 estimates from 54 studies can be used"; "To account for within-study dependence we use standard errors clustered at the study level and wild bootstrap" (p.8). Table 3 Panel A does carry a study-weighted column ("Study," p.16) and Table 6 reports a full weighted-mean (WM) column (pp.21-22). But the headline Table 8 fitted values set covariates to unweighted "sample means" (p.27), and the BMA (Table 7, p.25) runs on the 2,434 estimate-level observations.
- **Why it matters:** Clustered SEs widen intervals but do not correct point estimates or BMA posterior means for the fact that a handful of estimate-rich studies (visible in Figure 1, e.g., Altinok & Kingdon, Hojo) dominate the likelihood. The paper shows study-weighting matters ("giving each study the same weight results in more substantial estimates," p.13) but does not carry that into the bottom-line construction, and reports no leave-one-study-out/jackknife on the most prolific studies. (Narrower than "dependence never reaches the estimand," since the publication-bias machinery does include study weights — but the *implied-effect* estimand uses unweighted means.)

## Minority report

I preserve one grounded dissent against any consensus that treats the STAR-as-anomaly move as merely a tone problem to soften. The single best-identified study in the corpus reports -1.99 [-2.19, -1.78] (Table 2, p.12) — tight and large — survives every bias correction by the authors' own admission ("a robust feature of the STAR experiment data, not an artifact," p.30), and is assigned PIP 1.00, posterior mean -1.52 in BMA (Table 7, p.25). If the designs estimate genuinely different estimands, then the abstract's "negligible effect" is not a softer-or-stronger version of one truth but a **category error**: pooling a randomized LATE for young children in ~15-student classes with selection-on-observables associations elsewhere and declaring the pooled mean negligible. On this reading the paper's central interpretive thesis — not just its phrasing — is unsupported, and the burden the paper places on the observational consensus to overturn the one clean identification is never discharged with an identification argument.

## Conventional-referee assumption check

A conventional JLE referee would likely (i) accept the publication-bias contribution as the paper's real and novel value-add — no prior class-size meta-analysis corrected for selection (p.2), the funnel is genuinely near-symmetric (Figure 3, p.14), and ten-plus estimators converge; (ii) treat the 2,819/66 vs 2,434/54 distinction and the Table 2 subject overcount as non-issues or trivial copy-edits; (iii) read "negligible" charitably as describing the point estimate, not as a precision claim; and (iv) find the STAR-as-anomaly framing persuasive on the strength of the Lang (2025) t-stat argument and the genuine non-replication record. This audit accepts (i) and (ii) but contests (iii) and (iv): the conventional reading silently grants that a pooled mean across non-comparable identification strategies is a coherent estimand, and that low power can be used to discount the one well-identified positive result without symmetrically widening uncertainty about the null. Those are the load-bearing assumptions a methodological referee should not wave through. The conventional referee would also likely not notice the Table 4 printed-CI integrity error (criticism 5), which a numerical-accuracy pass catches.

## Rubric assessment (six equally-weighted dimensions)

- **Identification credibility — Weak-to-fair.** The publication-bias identification is reasonable and multiply triangulated. The *effect-magnitude* identification is the soft spot: the corrected mean is a conditional prediction at a chosen point, the orthogonality assumption behind the linear/selection corrections fails its own test (Table 4) and is assumed to be heterogeneity without a test, and the pooling of randomized, RDD, IV, FE, and (admittedly non-causal) OLS estimands into one "underlying effect" is never defended as targeting a common parameter.
- **Statistical validity — Fair.** Wild-bootstrap clustered inference (Roodman et al.), RVE, and a broad estimator suite are appropriate. But Table 8's intervals attach frequentist delta-method variance to BMA points, discarding model-averaging uncertainty, and the dependence correction does not reach the bottom-line fitted values.
- **Robustness coverage — Strong.** Five linear/IV/weighting specifications plus five nonlinear models (WAAP, Stem, Kink, p-uniform*, Selection), the Andrews & Kasy specification test, Elliott et al. p-curve tests, BMA with dilution prior plus BRIC/beta-binomial and frequentist-Mallows checks, and alternative effect-size definitions. Coverage is well above field norm; the main gap is that much of it lives in an external Online Appendix B not in this file, asserted to be "broadly in line" but unverified here.
- **Numerical / citation accuracy — Fair, one real defect.** Most cells reconcile (444+1,288+702 = 2,434; estimation block sums exactly). But Table 4 prints upper CI bounds below their point estimates in three of six rows (p.18) — a genuine integrity error. The subject-count "overcount" is not an error (means sum to 1.17). The data-dimension counts in Table 2 (402 + 2,026 = 2,428) fall 6 short of 2,434 despite Table 6 means partitioning to 1.00 — a small unreconciled gap, Low severity.
- **Transparency of limitations — Strong.** The paper runs and reports a falsifiable test against its own preferred estimator (Table 4), repeatedly flags the wide CI (p.27), discloses the full conditioning recipe (p.27), labels Table 8 CIs "approximate" (p.28), and concedes STAR survives every correction (p.30). This candor is the paper's strongest feature and lets a reader reconstruct the alternative reading.
- **Severity-weighted issue count — Moderate.** Four High issues, all converging on a single root (imprecise, assumption-fragile, conditional estimates translated into a confident null and a strong policy verdict), plus three Medium issues. The convergence is real signal: the publication-bias claim is robust; the negligible-effect claim and its policy payload are not.

## Action list

1. **Report the MAIVE corrected mean and CI explicitly** alongside Table 3, and state in one sentence whether the "little bias" *and the corrected magnitude* survive under the orthogonality-free estimators (MAIVE, Elliott et al.). Do not leave the reader to infer it from the IV-column constant.
2. **Replace the unargued "heterogeneity not bias" attribution** of the Table 4 correlation with a test that distinguishes the two (e.g., whether the correlation persists within homogeneous method-by-context cells).
3. **Re-typeset Table 4** so every point estimate falls within its bootstrap CI; confirm whether the upper bounds are transposed digits; report the bootstrap replication count and seed.
4. **Re-word "negligible"** to "statistically indistinguishable from zero but imprecisely estimated," and print the upper CI bound beside the point estimate wherever "negligible" appears (abstract, conclusion). State Table 8's overall mean as a conditional prediction at a stated best-practice point, not an average treatment effect.
5. **Present STAR vs. the quasi-experimental pool as observationally equivalent given the data**, or test the observational null within the STAR-comparable stratum (small classes, primary/kindergarten, US). Apply the Lang (2025) power bar symmetrically to the near-zero quasi-experimental estimates whose CIs are equally wide.
6. **Hedge the carved-out exceptions** (small classes, disadvantaged students): note their CIs include zero and report the subgroup n where the disadvantaged-student claim is made (n=166).
7. **Carry study-level weighting into the headline construction** (study-weighted covariate means or inverse-count weights) as a primary or co-equal specification, and add a leave-one-study-out / jackknife on the most prolific studies to show the null is not composition-driven.
8. **Replace Table 8's frequentist delta-method intervals** with posterior-predictive intervals from the BMA that retain model-averaging uncertainty, since that is the uncertainty the paper exists to quantify.
9. **Report inter-coder reliability** for the preferred/neutral/discounted hand-coding, and show the headline with "Preferred" excluded from both BMA and the best-practice conditioning, to address the label-endogeneity concern at low cost.
