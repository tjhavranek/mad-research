# MAD-research final memo
## Verdict
The paper’s empirical contribution is credible as a large hand-coded synthesis, but its identification credibility is weakened by the way pooled claims travel across OLS, fixed effects, IV, RDD, and STAR evidence. Statistical validity is the main pressure point: the paper reports a failed diagnostic for a central selection-model assumption and then still uses reassuring publication-bias language. Robustness coverage is broad in name, but the most policy-facing fitted values depend on hypothetical settings, weighting choices, and support conditions that are not fully auditable in the manuscript. Numerical accuracy has at least one clear internal problem in Table 4, while broader citation-verification claims remain [EXTERNAL]. Overall, this is not a fatal audit, but it supports a major-revision verdict: the dataset and toolkit are strong, while the abstract, Table 8 interpretation, STAR framing, and policy punchline overstate what the evidence can carry.

## Top surviving criticisms (5-7, by severity)
1. Categorical “negligible effect” claims outrun Table 8 uncertainty; Severity: High; Evidence “The implied class size effect is negligible for all identification approaches except Tennessee's Student/Teacher Achievement Ratio project and for all contexts except classes of fewer than 15 students” (Abstract, p.1); also “Overall corrected mean | -0.21 | -1.38, 0.97” and “Confidence intervals for implied effects are approximate” (Table 8, p.28). Why it matters: the abstract turns fitted central estimates with wide approximate intervals into all-method and all-context conclusions. The defensible statement is that central fitted values are small in many reported scenarios, not that the evidence establishes negligible effects across the claimed scope. Supporting audits: Y Round 2, Z Round 1, Z Round 2.

2. The publication-bias conclusion underplays a failed identifying-assumption diagnostic; Severity: High; Evidence “Table 4 shows the results for various subsets of data: we always find a substantial correlation. The finding indicates that the zero correlation assumption is tenuous” (Section 3, p.19); against “any potential publication bias in the literature taken as a whole appears weak” (Section 3, p.19). Why it matters: the paper is transparent about the problem, but the conclusion still reads as if triangulation resolved it. A failed orthogonality check should be propagated into the strength of the claim, not treated mainly as a caveat. Supporting audits: X Round 1, X Round 2, Y Round 2, Z Round 1, Z Round 2.

3. The pooled estimand mixes designs with different causal credibility; Severity: High; Evidence “The meta-analysis mean can be misleading if different identification approaches lead to systematically different results” and “The first approach is unlikely to succeed in recovering the causal estimate” (Section 1, p.5); later, “These studies collectively suggest a negligible class size effect” (Section 5, p.29-30). Why it matters: the manuscript itself says OLS is not a credible causal design, yet pooled publication-bias and BMA claims still help support a general effect statement. Method dummies reduce but do not eliminate the problem that different designs may target different estimands in different populations. Supporting audits: X Round 1, X Round 2, Z Round 2.

4. Table 8’s “best practice” fitted values are hypothetical and under-specified; Severity: Moderate-to-high; Evidence “we plug in zero for the standard error,” “OLS = 0,” “featured in an outlet with a high impact factor,” and “Other variables are set to their sample means” (Section 4, p.27); Table 8 says it “constructs hypothetical studies” (Table 8 notes, p.28). Why it matters: zero standard error, preferred estimates, recent/highly cited/high-impact publication status, and mixed sample means may describe no observed study. The fitted values are useful summaries, but they should not be presented as corrected context-level effects without the full covariate vector, support checks, and interval formula. Supporting audits: X Round 1, Y Round 1, Y Round 2, Z Round 2.

5. Weighting and within-study dependence affect the substantive headline, not just standard errors; Severity: Moderate; Evidence “within-study variation in results is large and often larger than variation in mean results across studies” (Section 2, p.8); Table 2 reports “All estimates | 2,434 | -0.25 ... | -0.72” under estimate-weighted versus study-weighted means (Table 2, p.13). Why it matters: clustered standard errors and wild bootstrap address inference, but they do not settle whether studies or estimates are the right unit for the headline estimand. The more negative study-weighted mean should be reconciled with the “near zero” framing, especially because studies contribute very different numbers of estimates. Supporting audits: X Round 1.

6. The STAR and policy language exceeds the evidence scope; Severity: Moderate; Evidence “the choice is between one experimental dataset and dozens of quasi-experimental ones” and “We find it more likely that the STAR experiment was an anomaly” (Section 5, p.30); also “substantial costs of class size reductions that we do not focus on in this paper” (Section 2, p.7) versus “class size reduction remains an evidence-based policy in search of evidence” (Section 5, p.30). Why it matters: the analysis concerns included test-score estimates, not a full welfare analysis, and it cannot fully adjudicate whether STAR is anomalous, context-specific, or better identified. The policy conclusion should be narrower. Supporting audits: X Round 2, Z Round 1, Z Round 2.

7. Table 4 contains an internal numerical reporting problem; Severity: Moderate but immediate; Evidence “Correlation | 0.555 [0.413, 0.537] | 0.835 [0.606, 0.816] | 0.609 [0.369, 0.599]” and “Bootstrapped 95% confidence intervals in parentheses” (Table 4, p.18). Why it matters: if the brackets are confidence intervals, the point estimate should not fall outside them. This may be a reporting or formatting error, but it directly affects a table central to the selection-model diagnostic. Supporting audits: Y Round 1; acknowledged as valid but lower-scope by Z Round 2.

## Minority report
A grounded minority objection should be preserved: the paper may be too quick to downgrade the one randomized evidence base. The manuscript concedes that “It is possible that the quasi-experimental studies share a problem that collectively prevents them from identifying the true effect” (Section 5, p.30), yet then states that “the STAR experiment was an anomaly” (Section 5, p.30). A minority reader could reasonably weight internal validity more heavily and treat STAR as evidence of a real effect in its setting, while interpreting the rest of the literature as external-validity or implementation evidence rather than a replication failure.

## Conventional-referee assumption check
A conventional referee might accept a many-estimate meta-analysis as stronger than a single experiment, but that assumption is only defensible when estimands and contexts are comparable. A conventional referee might also treat multiple publication-bias methods as mutually reinforcing; here, the paper’s own diagnostic says a key assumption is tenuous, so robustness breadth should not erase that caveat. Conversely, a conventional randomized-trial hierarchy should not automatically make STAR the general truth, because external validity remains open. The fair referee stance is to preserve the descriptive synthesis while forcing the causal, policy, and “all contexts” language to retreat.

## Action list
1. Rewrite the abstract and conclusion to say central fitted estimates are small in many contexts, with wide uncertainty and STAR as a major exception.
2. Make identification-stratified estimates the primary causal presentation; treat pooled estimates as descriptive.
3. Propagate the Table 4 diagnostic failure into the publication-bias conclusion, or provide bounds/sensitivity showing how much it could matter.
4. Report the full Table 8 covariate vector, support diagnostics, and interval formula; distinguish fitted predictions from causal effects.
5. Reconcile estimate-weighted and study-weighted results, including sensitivity to prolific studies.
6. Fix Table 4 confidence intervals or explain exactly what the brackets represent.
7. Narrow the policy and STAR language to test-score evidence and external validity, unless a cost-benefit framework is added.
