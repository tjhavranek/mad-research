# MAD-research final memo

## Verdict

The paper is credible as a meta-regression of *reported* hedge-fund alpha estimates, but less credible where it turns that design into investor-facing claims about current hedge-fund value. The strongest surviving issues are not arithmetic slips; they concern identification, selection correction, dependence among estimates, and labels placed on constructed quantities. Statistical validity is weakened by reliance on a “best practice” scenario whose inputs are partly judgmental and by BMA/PIP inference that is not shown under study-level dependence. Robustness coverage is good for BMA priors but thin for the more important scenario, publication-selection, fee, and time-trend choices. Numerical/citation accuracy problems are mostly secondary, though the fee and sample-size numerics materially affect interpretation. Overall, the severity-weighted issue count is concentrated in a few high-stakes overclaims rather than many small defects.

## Top surviving criticisms (5-7, by severity)

1. Headline shifts from reported estimates to actual hedge-fund value; Severity: High; Evidence “We examine the factors influencing published estimates of hedge fund performance” (Abstract, p.1; line 13) and “they do not do so anymore” (Conclusion, p.41; line 556); Why it matters: The design aggregates published alpha estimates and study characteristics, but the conclusion speaks as if actual hedge funds no longer create value. The paper itself gives the more cautious statistical statement: “we cannot reject the null hypothesis” (p.38; line 486). A zero-spanning interval supports non-detection of positive current alpha, not proof of no value. Supporting audits: X R1/R2, Z R1/R2, Y R2.

2. Publication-selection adjustment is under-shown but treated as eliminating bias; Severity: High; Evidence “we plug zero for the measure of a hedge fund alpha’s standard error” and “ensures that our best practices estimate is free of any publication selection bias” (p.37; line 474); Why it matters: The sample is explicitly published evidence, and publication selection is central to meta-analysis. Setting SE and SE×IV to zero may be a model-based adjustment, but the manuscript does not show the selection model or sensitivity sufficient to justify “free of” selection bias. [EXTERNAL] Standard funnel/PET/PEESE or selection-model checks are not substitutes for the authors’ claim unless they are reported in-paper. Supporting audits: X R1/R2, Y R2, Z R1.

3. The “current best practice” estimate depends on subjective scenario construction without enough scenario robustness; Severity: High; Evidence “the choice of these parameters inevitably involves a subjective judgment” (p.36; line 468), “set both the data year and the year of publication to the maximum values” (p.37; line 478), and “set the remaining variables to their sample means” (p.37; line 480); Why it matters: Table 4’s current-performance claim is conditional on an input vector not fully printed in the paper. Time variables are especially influential: “Data year: -0.149... | -0.802” and “Publication year” effects are discussed as large in Table 5 (p.39-40; lines 512, 520). The concern is not that the trend is false, but that the headline estimate is too central to leave without recent-window, jointly observed, nonlinear, and alternative-input sensitivity. Supporting audits: X R1/R2, Y R1, Z R2.

4. Fee contribution is labeled more strongly than the gross-net contrast supports; Severity: High; Evidence “our data allow us to estimate the mean management and performance fees charged by hedge funds” (Abstract, p.1; line 13), while Table 2 has “Net-of-fee returns: 984” and “Gross returns: 35” (p.21; lines 221-222); Why it matters: The coefficient is a contrast across reported alpha estimates, not necessarily a within-fund or within-study fee deduction. The manuscript says it can “quantify the effect of hedge fund fees” and concludes “Hedge funds seem to charge their investors more than 5.0% annually” (p.33; line 433). With only 35 gross-return observations and no shown paired design or common-support check, “gross-net reported-alpha gap” is the defensible label. Supporting audits: X R1, Y R2, Z R1/R2.

5. Dependence handling is incomplete for BMA/PIP inference; Severity: Moderate to high; Evidence “Observations: 1,019” and “Studies: 74” (Table 3, p.32; lines 424-425), while “The frequentist check (OLS)... is estimated using standard errors clustered at the study level” (p.32; line 427); Why it matters: The OLS check addresses clustered standard errors, but the headline BMA language relies on posterior inclusion probabilities. The paper interprets PIP cutoffs as “decisive” or “strong” (p.28-29; lines 336, 346), yet does not show PIPs under study-weighted, clustered-bootstrap, multilevel, or leave-one-study-out alternatives. This threatens precision and variable-selection confidence more than coefficient signs. Supporting audits: X R1/R2, Y R2.

6. Time-trend interpretation risks mixing fund performance decline with changing measurement practice; Severity: Moderate; Evidence “studies based on newer datasets and studies published more recently tend to report substantially lower hedge fund alpha estimates” followed by “hedge fund performance has substantially decreased over time” (p.34; line 445); Why it matters: The paper controls for research-design variables additively, but newer studies may differ systematically in fee treatment, databases, bias correction, and model choice. Without fixed-design subsamples or time-by-method interactions, the result is safest as a decline in reported alphas, not necessarily a clean structural decline in hedge-fund skill. Supporting audits: X R2, Z R2.

7. Policy and mechanism claims outrun the demonstrated mechanism evidence; Severity: Moderate; Evidence “important implications... for regulators” and “likely related to the intensity of competition... and the impact of the regulatory requirements” (Conclusion, p.41; line 556), followed by “more research to distinguish between these potential underlying causes” (p.42; line 560); Why it matters: The authors are right to list plausible mechanisms, but the manuscript does not identify competition, regulation, scale, transparency, or market efficiency as causes of the trend. The caveat helps, but the earlier language still reads more identified than the evidence. Supporting audits: Z R1/R2, X R2.

## Minority report

A grounded minority objection worth preserving is Audit X’s concern about the bear-market inference. Evidence: Table 2 reports “Bull market: 39” and “Bear market: 39” (p.21; lines 227-228), while the text concludes “hedge funds generate substantially lower alphas when stock market prices decline” and “do not seem to hold... ‘market-neutral’” positions (p.33; lines 435-437). This is not as central as the headline current-alpha claim, but the behavioral interpretation is strong relative to a small labeled subsample. A within-study bull/bear comparison would be a cleaner basis for that language.

## Points rejected under scrutiny

| Claim | Reason |
|---|---|
| The current-performance result is proven to be outside joint support and “manufactured.” | The manuscript directly supports concern about setting both time variables to maxima, but no quoted passage establishes that the joint covariate combination is outside observed support. |
| The time-trend scale issue is a high-severity flaw. | Table A1 defines the transformed “Data year” variable, and the prose could be clearer, but this is mainly a reporting/marginal-effects issue unless recalculation shows substantive error. |
| Monthly-to-annual wording is a major threat. | The 4.4% versus 4.32% convention issue is real but low-severity presentation cleanup, not a core identification problem. |
| CISDM “overstate” language is a major surviving criticism. | The sentence says “relative to studies based on the other databases” (p.34; line 439), which partly supplies the comparative benchmark. Softer wording would help, but this is not central. |
| Duplicate citation suffixes and bare replication URL materially undermine the paper. | These are worth fixing, but they are copyediting/traceability defects unless external verification shows replication failure. |
| Regulatory implications should be rejected entirely. | The authors explicitly call for more research on mechanisms, so the fair criticism is overstatement, not that the paragraph has no caveat. |
| BMA definitely treats all estimates as independent and inflates PIPs. | The manuscript does not show a dependence-aware BMA, so the robustness gap survives; the stronger claim of demonstrated inflation is not proven from the text alone. |

## Conventional-referee assumption check

A conventional referee may accept the paper’s core contribution because it has a large literature sample, a clear variable dictionary, BMA prior sensitivity, and study-clustered OLS checks. That charitable reading supports the descriptive claim that reported hedge-fund alphas have declined and that several study-design features move reported estimates. The adversarial audit changes the emphasis: the fragile parts are the marketed estimands, especially “current performance,” publication-selection removal, and fee estimation. The paper can remain publishable if it narrows the claims and adds targeted robustness where the headline conclusions actually depend on maintained assumptions.

## Action list

1. Rewrite abstract, introduction, and conclusion to say “reported alphas” and “we cannot reliably document positive current net-of-fee alpha,” not “they do not do so anymore.”
2. Add a full Table 4 input-vector table and sensitivity to recent windows, medians versus means, maxima versus observed current profiles, and nonlinear time terms.
3. Report in-paper publication-selection diagnostics and sensitivity to the zero-SE assumption; label [EXTERNAL] methods such as FAT-PET/PEESE or selection models if used.
4. Add dependence-aware robustness for BMA: study-weighted BMA, cluster bootstrap, multilevel meta-regression, and leave-one-study-out checks for PIPs.
5. Reframe the fee result as a gross-net reported-alpha gap unless within-study gross/net pairs or common-support checks support a fee interpretation.
6. Recast competition, regulation, and market-efficiency statements as hypotheses unless mechanism-specific evidence is added.
7. For bear-market claims, report paired within-study bull/bear comparisons or soften the market-neutrality interpretation.
