# MAD-research final memo (Claude-only arm)

Paper: *Meta-Analysis of Field Studies on Beauty and Professional Success* (Irsova, Havranek, Bortnikova, Bartoš), draft dated December 1, 2025. PDF verified page-by-page against the three audits.

## Verdict

This is a technically strong, unusually transparent meta-analysis whose publication-bias machinery (z-curve, caliper tests, RoBMA, MAIVE, RTMA, PET–PEESE, three-level dependence models) is genuinely state-of-the-art and well triangulated, and whose descriptive backbone — a raw premium of about 4.3–5% that falls by at least a third after bias correction — is reproducible from the tables. The defensible problems are concentrated at the *interpretation and identification* layer, not the computation layer, and they converge on a single root cause: the paper repeatedly states correlational, between-study results in causal language. The two marquee claims both outrun the design: "controlling for cognitive ability renders the premium small" rests on a *between-study moderator* (a dummy for whether a primary study happened to include an ability control), not a within-subject adjustment, and the paper itself concedes ability "could act as a mediator rather than a confounder" (p.13), which would make conditioning on it a bad control. The anti-taste-discrimination verdict leans on an "earnings ≈ productivity" comparison whose productivity proxies (teaching evaluations, electoral success) are observer-rated and plausibly carry the very halo channel the test is meant to exclude. Several numerical and framing slips are real and verified — most seriously a text-vs-table credible interval on the one positive subgroup (sex workers) and a verbal "IV ≈ OLS" claim that the paper's own Table S2 contradicts in sign. None of these sink the descriptive findings, but the causal headline and the abstract's most-quotable numbers are stronger than the evidence licenses.

## Top surviving criticisms (by severity)

### 1. The cognitive-ability result is a between-study moderator contrast, not a within-subject adjustment — yet it is the load-bearing input to the central causal claim
- **Severity: High.**
- **Evidence:** The adjustment enters as a study-level dummy, not a person-level covariate: Table 3 reports "Cognitive skill control" P. mean −2.265, PIP 1.000, defined as "the partial derivative of the reported beauty premium with respect to the corresponding study characteristic" (Table 3 notes, p.33), and the text reads "controlling for cognitive ability in the primary study or using difference-in-differences makes the remaining premium small (mean = 1.1% ...)" (p.2). Table 2 contrasts non-overlapping study sets — RoBMA "Cognitive skill control only 2.03 [−2.77, −0.62]" vs "No skill control 1.64 [0.75, 2.45]" — and the notes concede subgroup means "need not average to the overall mean" because each "use[s] only estimates for that dimension" (Table 2 notes, p.31).
- **Why it matters:** "Controlling for ability" in the abstract reads as conditioning on a common ability variable for the same workers. It is not: it compares the beauty coefficient in studies that happened to include an IQ-type control against studies that did not. Those study sets are not exchangeable (longitudinal cohort designs vs cross-sectional wage regressions differ in era, sample, outcome), so the −2.3-pp gap confounds "adding an ability control" with "being the kind of study that has ability data." The BMA conditions on 35 covariates, which blunts but does not remove this — adjustment is only for *observed* design features. The fragility shows in the paper's own robustness row: restricting to objective ability or DID, "Objective cog. control or DID" is RoBMA 0.06 [0.00, 0.86] (Table 2, p.31; Table S3, p.47), again a different small subset, not a within-study decomposition.

### 2. The headline "beauty is not important per se" assumes confounding, but the paper concedes ability may be a mediator, leaving the policy-relevant total effect unidentified
- **Severity: High.**
- **Evidence:** Abstract/intro: "These findings suggest that beauty is not important in the labor market per se but via its correlation with other characteristics" (p.2); abstract "two factors combine to explain away most of the mean beauty premium" (p.1). Discussion: "Under this developmental framework, cognitive ability could act as a mediator rather than a confounder; thus, while we do not find strong evidence of substantial direct discrimination, beauty could remain economically significant as a driver of human capital acquisition that the labor market later rewards" (p.13), reinforced by "If beauty maps into schooling and measured ability through such early reinforcement, conditioning on those factors should largely absorb the premium, as we find" (p.13).
- **Why it matters:** If beauty → schooling/ability → wages, ability is a post-treatment mediator and conditioning on it removes part of the *total* effect (over-control / bad control), rather than identifying "no effect." The paper's own developmental story (attractive children "complete more education," p.13) actively supports the mediator reading. The two readings imply opposite policy conclusions for the anti-discrimination/accident-compensation stakes the intro raises (p.2). The p.13 caveat does not cure an abstract that states the opposite without qualification; abstracts travel independently. Compounds criticism 1: even granting a clean within-study contrast, the *interpretation* would still be unidentified.

### 3. Text-vs-table contradiction on the sex-worker credible interval — on the paper's single positive result
- **Severity: High (within numerical-accuracy lane); material because it lands on the one carved-out exception.**
- **Evidence:** Main text: "For sex workers, the implied premium is 5.4% (95% CrI = 2.3, 10.5)" (p.12). Table 4 Part 2 reports "Prostitutes 5.39 [2.28, 8.50]" with the adjacent row "Politicians 1.06 [−8.41, 10.52]" (Table 4, p.34). The text's upper bound (10.5) matches the Politicians row (10.52), not Prostitutes (8.50); the lower bound 2.3 ≈ 2.28 does match Prostitutes. Part 4 reports a third, different sex-worker interval, "Prostitutes 1.42 [−4.05, 10.20]."
- **Why it matters:** The sex-worker premium is the abstract's sole exception ("small ... for all occupations except sex workers," p.1). An interval that does not match its own source row, and that coincides with an adjacent row's bound, signals a transcription error precisely where the paper's one significant subgroup result lives. Compounded by the fact that the Table 4 "implied premium" is itself a hand-calibrated split-normal construction (see criticism 7).

### 4. The "earnings ≈ productivity ⇒ not taste-based discrimination" inference rests on an untested assumption that productivity proxies are appearance-clean — which the paper's own proxy list violates
- **Severity: Medium–High.**
- **Evidence:** Abstract: "The beauty premium is similar for earnings and productivity, a fact inconsistent with discrimination based on employer tastes for beauty" (p.1); "If the beauty premium ... was due to taste-based discrimination by employers, we would expect a larger correlation of beauty with earnings than with productivity" (p.2). But the "productivity" outcomes include observer-rated measures: Table 3 lists "Teaching & research outcomes" and "Electoral success" under measurement of success (p.33), and Table S2 reports "Electoral success" mean 7.00 (p.46). The inference is never run on objectively measured output alone, and the moderator null on output measurability (Table 2 RoBMA all 0.19; RoBMA-3lvl 3.15/3.15/3.15, p.31) shows the contamination is *not* being differenced out.
- **Why it matters:** This is the paper's main *novel* anti-taste claim. If "productivity" is partly evaluator-rated, equality of earnings and productivity premiums is consistent *with* customer/employer taste-based discrimination, inverting the conclusion. The identifying assumption is stated only as the adjective "(imperfect)" (p.2), never as a testable condition.

### 5. The verbal "IV ≈ OLS / no attenuation bias" claim is contradicted in sign by the paper's own Table S2 summary statistics
- **Severity: Medium.**
- **Evidence:** Text asserts similarity: "We find no evidence consistent with substantial attenuation bias: IV estimates tend to be similar to OLS estimates" (p.5); "We find no evidence that IV estimates differ systematically from OLS estimates" (p.14). But Table S2 (p.46), "Estimation technique," reports unweighted means "Ordinary least squares ... 4.17 [3.77, 4.56]" (903 est., 60 stud.) vs "Instrumental variables ... 4.86 [3.07, 6.64]" (83 est., 10 stud.), and weighted "5.19" (OLS) vs "6.60" (IV). IV exceeds OLS by 0.69 pp unweighted and 1.41 pp weighted — the direction (IV > OLS) is exactly what attenuation bias predicts, not "no difference."
- **Why it matters:** "No attenuation bias" is one of three abstract-level pillars ("We find little evidence of attenuation bias that could offset publication and omitted-variable biases," p.1). The unconditional contrast the text gestures at points the other way; the conditional BMA result that would rescue it (Table 3 "IV method" P. mean −0.001, PIP 0.005) is never named as the sentence's basis, and the diagnostic rests on only 10 IV studies / 83 estimates with no power statement. The OLS–IV proxy is also asked to do double duty: the paper argues IV in this literature is "unlikely to help with omitted variables or reverse causality" (p.14) to license the attenuation reading, while elsewhere noting IV "can also help with other endogeneity issues" (p.21) — so an OLS≈IV finding could equally reflect offsetting biases.

### 6. "Small for all occupations except sex workers" presents an economy-weighted scalar as per-occupation uniformity
- **Severity: Medium.**
- **Evidence:** Abstract: "controlling for cognitive ability renders the premium small (mean = 1.1%; 95% CrI = −0.8%, 3.0%) for all occupations except sex workers" (p.1). But 1.1% is the aggregate: Table 4 labels "Occupation-weighted estimate 1.11 [−0.75, 3.00]," computed via "employment shares from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics' May 2023 ... Statistics" (p.26). Per-occupation RoBMA-3lvl fitted values in the same table are materially larger — "Scientists 1.58," "Athletes 1.53," "Politicians 1.30," "Executives 1.29" (Table 4 Part 4, p.34) — and the corresponding RoBMA-3lvl subgroup means in Table 2 sit at 3.3–4.8% with upper credible bounds near 5–7% (p.31).
- **Why it matters:** The most-quoted sentence attaches a single small aggregate to "all occupations," which reads as "every occupation's premium is ~1%." The subgroup tables do not show that. A defender can note every non-sex-work interval includes zero, so the *statistical* claim ("not distinguishable from small") holds; but the abstract asserts *magnitude* ("the premium [is] small"), and several point estimates with upper bounds to 7% are not obviously small. The slippage between "indistinguishable from small" and "is small" is not flagged.

### 7. The headline 1.1% is a hand-calibrated split-normal projection, not an estimated parameter
- **Severity: Medium.**
- **Evidence:** Methods: "We estimated the economy-wide distribution using a mixture of split-normal distributions weighted by occupational shares. To ensure internal consistency, the location parameters of the split-normal distributions were adjusted such that the expected value of each subgroup's simulation matched the reported meta-analytic mean exactly, while preserving the reported asymmetry of the credible intervals" (p.26). BMA fitted values are computed at non-representative settings — beauty "software-rated or assessed by the maximum number of raters," other characteristics "set to their maximum observed values" (p.26). Table 4 notes its intervals are "approximated using uncertainty estimated in the frequentist model," i.e., not a posterior.
- **Why it matters:** The abstract's most-quotable statistic is two modeling layers (BLS reweighting + split-normal pinning at extreme covariate settings) removed from any estimator output, projected onto a workforce whose occupations are demonstrably unrepresentative of the studied sample (students = 233 estimates/9 studies; general population = 435/23). The point is *constructed* to reproduce subgroup means, then presented as the population beauty premium with a credible interval implying direct identification. This is a transparency/altitude problem, fully disclosed in Methods but not in the abstract.

## Minority report

**Preserved grounded dissent (sharpened from Audit Z):** The paper's positioning claim — that it provides "independent evidence against employer taste-based discrimination" by aligning itself with Stinebrickner et al. and Nault et al. (p.3) — is internally inconsistent and should not survive review unrevised, independently of the abstract's wording. Stinebrickner et al. is cited as showing "the beauty premium exists only in occupations where interpersonal interaction is important" (p.3) and as finding "no beauty effects for jobs where employees do not come into personal contact with customers" (p.14) — the canonical signature of a customer/taste channel. Yet this paper's own moderator analysis finds *no* interpersonal-intensity gradient: RoBMA-3lvl low/mid/high = 3.14/3.16/3.16 and "Interpersonal intensity" P. mean 0.000, PIP 0.006 (Table 2 p.31; Table 3 p.32). The paper attributes its own null to "the limited occupational granularity available in primary studies" (p.13) — but if that excuse is taken seriously, it equally undercuts the "independent evidence" the cited study is marshaled to support. The contribution is thus positioned as corroborating a literature it partially contradicts. This is a positioning/identification defect, not a wording fix, and it deserves to survive even if the panel treats the mediator issue as merely rhetorical.

## Conventional-referee assumption check

A standard referee would likely accept three premises this audit flags as load-bearing and contestable:

1. **That "controlling for cognitive ability" in a meta-regression is equivalent to conditioning on ability.** It is not — it is a study-characteristic dummy comparing non-overlapping study sets (criticism 1). A referee skimming the abstract would not catch that the causal verb sits on a between-study correlation.
2. **That a null moderator on O*NET interpersonal intensity / output measurability is informative about the discrimination mechanism.** The indices are occupation-average task content mapped onto worker-level estimates (binned at author-chosen cutoffs, e.g. the "0.4 ... natural break," Table S18), so any true gradient is mechanically attenuated toward the null by aggregation; a flat profile cannot bear the interpretive weight the Discussion places on it (p.13). Continuous-moderator robustness is absent.
3. **That MAIVE's instrument is "strong by definition."** The paper asserts "Sample size is a strong instrument by virtue of the definition of the standard error" (p.22) and reports no sample-specific first-stage F or partial-R² anywhere in 72 pages, despite otherwise extensive diagnostics; "marginal statistical insignificance of publication bias" is then used to license a specific corrected point (3.1) rather than reported as inconclusive.

One genuinely *strong* feature a skeptical referee should credit, and which this audit endorses: the paper does **not** hide its own counter-evidence. It flags the mediator alternative (p.13), and — to its considerable credit — openly reports that its selection-model identifying assumption appears violated: "estimates and standard errors are correlated even after correction for publication bias ... the assumption of no relation between estimates and standard errors in the absence of publication bias is by far the most important assumption" (p.23; Table S4 correlation 0.550 [0.43, 0.613]). Disclosing the failure of your key assumption is rare and raises the transparency verdict materially.

## Rubric verdicts (prose, six equally-weighted dimensions)

**Identification credibility — Weak.** This is the paper's soft underbelly. The central causal narrative pivots on cognitive ability being a confounder, but the paper never commits to a causal diagram or a target estimand, conditions on what it concedes may be a mediator (p.13), and operationalizes "control" as a between-study moderator rather than a within-subject adjustment (criticisms 1–2). The anti-taste inference depends on an untested clean-proxy assumption its own data violate (criticism 4). The descriptive premium is well identified; the causal headline is not.

**Statistical validity — Mixed, leaning adequate.** The estimators are appropriate and modern, dependence is modeled at the study/database level rather than papered over with clustered SEs, and the bias-correction inference is defensible. But two genuine validity concerns persist: the selection model's key assumption is shown to fail on the paper's own diagnostic (Table S4, p.23), and several null findings (attenuation bias, interpersonal gradient) are accepted on thin data (10 IV studies) with no minimum-detectable-effect or power statement, converting "absence of evidence" into "evidence of absence."

**Robustness coverage — Strong.** This is the paper's best dimension. It varies the estimator (RoBMA, RoBMA-3lvl, RoBMA-3lvl-database, selection model, PET–PEESE, MAIVE, RTMA), the unit of analysis (estimate/study/database), and the effect metric (percent vs standardized), reports them side by side (Table 1), adds z-curve, caliper, and funnel diagnostics (Figure 3, Tables S5–S6), and supplies a frequentist stepwise mirror of the Bayesian model (Table 3). The one missing robustness piece is continuous (rather than binned) moderators for the mechanism tests.

**Numerical/citation accuracy — Mixed.** Most numbers reconcile and N's tie out (688 + 471 = 1,159; 4 sex-work studies / 55 estimates; corrected mean 2.9 ≈ one-third below 4.3; MAIVE 3.05 ≈ 3.1). But there is one verified High-severity text-vs-table contradiction on the sex-worker interval (p.12 vs Table 4, p.34), one verified overstatement ("identical ... 4.3%" for earnings vs productivity when Table S2 shows 4.33 vs 4.20 unweighted and 6.15 vs 3.74 weighted, p.46), and one verbal claim contradicted in sign by the paper's own table ("IV ≈ OLS," p.5/p.14 vs Table S2, p.46). These are correctable but real.

**Transparency of limitations — Strong.** Notably above field norm. The paper states the mediator alternative that undercuts its own headline (p.13), labels productivity proxies "(imperfect)," discloses the split-normal calibration procedure (p.26), reports that its selection-model assumption is violated (p.23), brackets the sex-worker subsample as non-representative (p.18), and concedes "we cannot fully correct for what almost certainly is an attenuation bias" (p.14). The gap is that these caveats live in the body while the abstract states the opposite without qualification.

**Severity-weighted issue count — Moderate.** Three High-severity issues (between-study moderator misframing; confounder-vs-mediator; sex-worker interval), four Medium (clean-proxy assumption; IV≈OLS sign contradiction; "all occupations" overclaim; constructed 1.1%), plus one preserved minority objection (Stinebrickner positioning). Critically, none is a computational error in the bias-correction layer and none overturns the descriptive findings; all are fixable by relabeling quantities, adding diagnostics, or re-running existing comparisons. The cluster is interpretive, concentrated, and remediable — not fatal.

## Action list

1. **State the estimand.** Commit to a single causal diagram and distinguish the *total* effect of beauty from the *controlled direct* effect. Report the unconditional and ability-conditioned premiums side by side as bounding a mediation decomposition; stop interpreting the conditioned number as "the importance of beauty." Add a sensitivity analysis to mediator-outcome confounding.
2. **Relabel the cognitive-ability result.** Replace causal verbs ("renders," "explain away," "becomes small once controlled") with the accurate description: *studies that control for cognitive ability report a ~2.3-pp smaller premium.* Where the shared cohort datasets permit (refs 30–32), report a genuine within-study decomposition on the *same* sample.
3. **Fix the sex-worker interval.** Reconcile p.12 to Table 4 (upper bound 8.50, not 10.5), or correct Table 4; verify which specification the 5.4% figure is drawn from given Part 4 reports a third interval (1.42 [−4.05, 10.20]).
4. **Re-run the earnings-vs-productivity test on objectively measured output only** (sales, piece-rate), excluding teaching evaluations and electoral success; state the clean-proxy assumption explicitly as a testable condition.
5. **Correct the IV≈OLS and "identical 4.3%" statements.** Name the conditional BMA partial effect (≈0, PIP 0.005) as the basis for the attenuation conclusion and explicitly note the *unconditional* IV mean (4.86/6.60) exceeds OLS (4.17/5.19); replace "identical" with "similar (4.33 vs 4.20 unweighted)" and acknowledge the weighted gap. Add a minimum-detectable-effect statement for the 10-IV-study comparison.
6. **Demote the 1.1% in the abstract** from headline parameter to "illustrative occupation-weighted projection," flag its split-normal construction and extreme covariate settings, and reword "small for all occupations" to "the workforce-weighted average is small," separately noting per-occupation point estimates range higher with wide intervals.
7. **Report MAIVE instrument strength** (first-stage F / partial-R²) in the main text; present the AR result as "publication bias not rejected at 5%" without simultaneously committing to the single point 3.1.
8. **Treat mechanism moderators as continuous** (interpersonal intensity, output measurability), report sensitivity to the O*NET cutoffs, and acknowledge occupation-level aggregation as a limit on reading the null as evidence against a taste channel.
9. **Reframe the Stinebrickner positioning** as complementary-but-not-unanimous; do not claim "independent evidence against" a study whose key positive (interpersonal-only) finding this meta-analysis fails to reproduce.
10. **Add the PRISMA 1,715→500 exclusion box** and deposit a frozen, DOI'd, licensed snapshot alongside the project URL.
