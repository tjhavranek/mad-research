# MAD-research final memo
## Verdict
The manuscript is statistically ambitious and unusually broad in robustness coverage, especially on publication-bias correction and model uncertainty. The main surviving problem is identification credibility: the paper’s headline language treats a conditional residual premium as if it resolved the total causal role of beauty, while the manuscript itself allows cognitive ability to be downstream of beauty. Statistical validity is not fatally undermined, but the representative correction and unit-of-analysis choices need clearer justification. Numerical and citation accuracy require repair because several table references, subgroup labels, and adjusted-sample definitions are internally inconsistent. The severity-weighted issue count is moderate: two high-severity interpretive overclaims plus several fixable but central transparency and traceability problems.

## Top surviving criticisms (5-7, by severity)
1. Conditional residual premium is framed as the total beauty effect; Severity: High; Evidence "two factors combine to explain away most of the mean beauty premium" (p.1, manuscript_text.md:16); "beauty is not important in the labor market per se" (p.2, manuscript_text.md:30); "cognitive ability could act as a mediator rather than a confounder" (p.13, manuscript_text.md:152); Why it matters: If beauty affects schooling or measured ability, conditioning on cognitive ability estimates a residual/direct effect, not the total effect of beauty. The abstract and introduction should not imply that beauty itself has been explained away unless the paper separates confounding from mediation. Supporting audits: Audit X Round 1 and Round 2; Audit Z Round 1 and Round 2.

2. Earnings-productivity parity carries too much anti-discrimination weight; Severity: High; Evidence "The beauty premium is similar for earnings and productivity, a fact inconsistent with discrimination based on employer tastes for beauty" (p.1, manuscript_text.md:16); "Productivity in this context can be measured as sales, research outcomes, study outcomes, electoral success, etc." (p.18, manuscript_text.md:214); Why it matters: Many listed productivity proxies can themselves reflect customer, evaluator, voter, or institutional responses to appearance. Similarity between earnings and these proxies is suggestive, but it does not rule out taste-based or perception-based channels. Supporting audits: Audit X Round 1 and Round 2; Audit Z Round 1 and Round 2.

3. Policy and compensation framing exceeds the identified parameter; Severity: High; Evidence "The answers have consequences for anti-discriminatory legislation and compensations after accidents damaging looks" (p.2, manuscript_text.md:26); "little evidence of an additional payoff once information about ability is available" (p.13, manuscript_text.md:152); Why it matters: Legal discrimination and accident-compensation questions can depend on total losses, treatment channels, and pre-labor-market effects, not only the residual return after conditioning on ability. The manuscript may motivate policy relevance, but it should state that it does not estimate legal damages or adjudicate individual discrimination claims. Supporting audits: Audit Z Round 1 and Round 2; Audit X Round 1.

4. Representative publication-bias correction is not transparently aligned with dependence-aware estimation; Severity: Moderate; Evidence "the simple one that treats each estimate as the unit of analysis" and "the three-level one that treats each study as the unit" (p.9, manuscript_text.md:106); "simple RoBMA suggests a mean beauty premium of 0.2, while three-level RoBMA yields 3.1" (p.9, manuscript_text.md:110); "representative estimate we choose... 2.9 corresponding to the simple OLS" (p.23, manuscript_text.md:264); Why it matters: The paper acknowledges within-study dependence, but its representative correction is tied to an estimate-level funnel regression. Because the estimate-level and study-level corrected means differ sharply in RoBMA, the primary estimand should be justified by unit of analysis, not convenience or proximity to another estimator. Supporting audits: Audit X Round 1; Audit Y Round 2.

5. Publication-bias funnel assumptions are acknowledged as contaminated but still rhetorically central; Severity: Moderate; Evidence "some of the correlation between estimates and standard errors can plausibly be unrelated to publication bias" (p.22, manuscript_text.md:250); "funnel asymmetry tests introduce a downward bias that did not exist before" (p.22, manuscript_text.md:254); Why it matters: The manuscript has good alternative tools, including MAIVE and RTMA, but the prose still leans on a funnel-based representative number. This does not overturn the approximate corrected premium, because several methods cluster near it, but it weakens transparency about which identifying assumption is doing the work. Supporting audits: Audit X Round 1; Audit Y Round 2.

6. Internal table references and adjusted-sample definitions impair reproducibility; Severity: Moderate; Evidence "Panel A of Table S8" and "2.9 corresponding to the simple OLS" (p.23, manuscript_text.md:264); "Effect beyond bias (constant): OLS 2.865" (p.49, manuscript_text.md:689); "Objective cog. control or DID method 395/21" (p.46, manuscript_text.md:627); "random assignment, difference-in-differences, instrumental variables" (p.71, manuscript_text.md:1032); Why it matters: The 2.9 value appears in Table S6, not the cited Table S8, and the adjusted cognition/quasi-experimental sample is not consistently defined. These look fixable, but they affect central claims and prevent readers from tracing the reported 1.1% result cleanly. Supporting audits: Audit Y Round 1 and Round 2; Audit Z Round 2.

7. Subgroup language understates residual uncertainty; Severity: Moderate; Evidence "All individual contexts examined in Part 4 are consistent with small beauty premiums" (p.12, manuscript_text.md:142); "Scientists 1.58 [−0.35,7.29]; Prostitutes 1.42 [−4.05,10.20]" (p.34, manuscript_text.md:416); Why it matters: The point estimates are small, but some credible intervals still allow substantively larger context-specific effects. The manuscript should distinguish "small point estimates" from "intervals rule out large effects." Supporting audits: Audit Y Round 2; Audit Z Round 1.

## Points rejected under scrutiny
| Claim | Reason |
|---|---|
| The publication-bias correction is invalid because funnel assumptions fail. | Overstated. The manuscript itself flags funnel endogeneity and reports MAIVE, RTMA, three-level RoBMA, and other checks. The issue is presentation and estimator hierarchy, not invalidation. |
| The paper hides that it is limited to field studies. | Rejected. The title says field studies, and Methods explicitly says "We focus on studies conducted in field settings" and "we exclude laboratory experiments and surveys." |
| The authors can readily test mediator versus confounder status with existing timing codes. | Not established. The manuscript documents cognitive categories such as IQ, test scores, and grades, but not a pre-treatment versus contemporaneous timing variable. |
| Search reproducibility is a fatal high-severity flaw. | Demoted. It is a real transparency concern, but the manuscript gives enough search and exclusion information that it belongs in the minority report unless missing studies are shown to change results. |
| Nault et al. positioning is a central surviving flaw. | Too peripheral. The wording should be softened, but it does not materially affect the meta-analytic estimates or main adjusted claims. |
| [EXTERNAL] Bibliographic page-range errors should survive. | Rejected here because external bibliographic accuracy was not independently verified from the manuscript and audits alone. |

## Conventional-referee assumption check
A conventional referee might accept the cognitive-control result as ordinary omitted-variable adjustment and treat the mediator caveat as sufficient because it appears in the Discussion. The adversarial reading is stricter: the abstract, introduction, and policy framing are what most readers will cite, and those sections state a broader conclusion than the adjusted estimand supports. A conventional referee might also treat table-reference errors as production issues, but here they affect central traceability. The paper remains publishable in spirit if reframed as a strong conditional meta-analysis rather than a mechanism-resolving causal verdict.

## Action list
1. Rewrite the abstract and introduction to say the residual/direct premium is small conditional on publication-bias correction and cognitive controls, not that beauty is generally "explained away."
2. Define the target estimand explicitly: total effect, controlled direct effect, or descriptive adjusted association.
3. Move the mediator caveat into the abstract or first contribution paragraph, and state what evidence would be needed to separate mediation from confounding.
4. Recast earnings-productivity parity as suggestive evidence, and separate employer taste, customer taste, evaluator bias, and measured-productivity channels.
5. Make the study-level or database-level specification primary, or justify why an estimate-level OLS correction is the representative publication-bias number.
6. Correct the Table S6/S8 reference, harmonize earnings and non-earnings labels, and state exactly whether IV estimates enter the objective-cognition/DID/quasi-experimental adjusted sample.
7. Replace broad subgroup claims with language distinguishing small point estimates from wide credible intervals, and version the online data/code archive.
