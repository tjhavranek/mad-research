# MAD-research final memo
## Verdict
The paper is a valuable focused meta-analysis, but its headline interpretation runs ahead of what the design cleanly identifies. Identification credibility is the weakest criterion: the corrected means depend on treating estimate-standard error patterns as publication selection even though the manuscript admits alternative channels. Statistical validity is stronger than the rhetoric, because the paper triangulates correction methods and uses BMA for model uncertainty, but robustness coverage is incomplete for common support, field classification, winsorization, and study-level dependence. Numerical accuracy concerns are mostly about range communication and table interpretation, not clear arithmetic failure; citation issues remain minor or unverified. The limitations are unusually candid in the body, but they are not carried through the abstract and conclusion with the same force, so the severity-weighted issue count is concentrated in a few serious overclaims rather than a wholesale failure.

## Top surviving criticisms (5-7, by severity)
1. SE-based correction is not fully identified against heterogeneity and estimator-internal precision links; Severity: Severe; Evidence “because of heterogeneity” (Section 4, manuscript_text.md:191); Why it matters: the funnel slope and corrected intercept are interpreted as publication-bias correction, but the manuscript itself says estimate-standard error correlation can also reflect omitted-variable heterogeneity. BMA conditioning on observed moderators reduces the problem, but does not prove that unobserved heterogeneity or estimator mechanics are absent. [EXTERNAL] In weak-instrument GMM settings, point estimates and standard errors can move together for reasons internal to estimation, not reporting selection; the manuscript’s own Table 4 shows GMM is common. Supporting audits: Audit X Round 1 and Round 2; Audit Y Round 2.

2. Publication-bias language states a mechanism more strongly than the design can observe; Severity: Severe; Evidence “because of publication bias” (Abstract, manuscript_text.md:20); Why it matters: the design observes funnel asymmetry and model-based corrected means, not the publication process itself. The manuscript also says publication bias and p-hacking are observationally equivalent and that correction methods have limitations, so “underlying risk aversion” should be framed as a conditional benchmark rather than a directly identified structural truth. Supporting audits: Audit X Round 2; Audit Z Round 1; Audit Y Round 2.

3. Table 6 turns subjective extrapolation into calibration advice; Severity: Serious; Evidence “We plug zero” (Section 4, manuscript_text.md:209); Why it matters: the recommended values are fitted from a constructed “best practice” bundle: zero standard error, sample maxima for some variables, chosen preference weights, and sample means for others. The manuscript says the exercise is subjective and imprecise, but the conclusion presents the output as values recommended for calibration. Without leverage or common-support diagnostics, the calibration advice rests on an extrapolated prediction rather than a demonstrated empirical region. Supporting audits: Audit X Round 1 and Round 2; Audit Y Round 2; Audit Z Round 1.

4. The finance recommendation compresses the paper’s own correction uncertainty; Severity: Serious; Evidence “2–7 in finance contexts” (Abstract, manuscript_text.md:20); Why it matters: Table 3 reports finance corrected values spanning low and high alternatives, including the p-uniform* and Andrews-Kasy results. The body acknowledges that individual results vary, but the abstract and conclusion convert the evidence into a narrower practical range without a decision rule for excluding or discounting the tails. This is a communication and robustness problem, not a reason to discard the qualitative finance finding. Supporting audits: Audit Y Round 1; Audit Z Round 2; Audit X Round 2.

5. The calibration-estimate wedge compares non-parallel objects; Severity: Serious; Evidence “published papers” (Data section, manuscript_text.md:58); Why it matters: the estimate sample includes published and unpublished work, while the calibration sample is published-only and stops after 200 usable Google Scholar-ranked studies. The conclusion then compares a publication-bias-corrected estimate distribution with an uncorrected calibration distribution. That descriptive wedge is useful, but it is not a clean common-estimand comparison and should not by itself justify recommended calibration values. Supporting audits: Audit X Round 1; Audit Z Round 2.

6. The economics-versus-finance split is used substantively despite a crude proxy; Severity: Moderate; Evidence “crude” (Conclusion, manuscript_text.md:331); Why it matters: the headline “economics” and “finance” values are central to the paper, but assignment is journal-category based rather than directly based on population, asset class, or estimation design. The paper defends the rule as clear, but robustness to alternative field definitions is not shown in the main text. The safer interpretation is “journal-category contrast” unless sensitivity checks support the broader field framing. Supporting audits: Audit Z Round 1; Audit Y Round 1.

7. Outlier handling is under-specified for a highly skewed outcome; Severity: Moderate; Evidence “winsorize estimates” (Table 1 note, manuscript_text.md:92); Why it matters: the manuscript does not state the exact cutoffs, whether winsorization is one- or two-sided, whether it is applied by field or full sample, or how key tables change without it. This matters because the paper itself notes standard-error outliers and because corrected means, funnel slopes, and fitted recommendations can move when extreme RRA estimates are handled differently. Supporting audits: Audit Y Round 1 and Round 2.

## Minority report
A grounded minority objection should be preserved: the abstract’s broad calibration claim conflicts with the field-specific body text. Evidence: “opposite is the case” (Data section, manuscript_text.md:68). The body says finance estimates overtop calibrations, while the abstract says calibrations are systematically larger than estimates; this is a lower-severity wording issue because the body later qualifies the pattern, but the abstract should not state the field-general version.

## Conventional-referee assumption check
A conventional referee might accept PET-style zero-standard-error extrapolation, BMA posterior inclusion probabilities, journal-field categories, and clustered standard errors as familiar tools in economics meta-analysis. That assumption is partly reasonable: the paper is transparent, uses multiple correction methods, and the qualitative finding of upward selective reporting is not fragile on the evidence reviewed here. The adversarial issue is narrower: standard practice does not by itself identify the exact corrected means as “underlying risk aversion” or justify direct calibration prescriptions. Most fixes are diagnostic and rhetorical rather than structural.

## Action list
1. Reword the abstract and conclusion: replace causal “because” and “driven by” language with “consistent with” and “associated with.”
2. Report SE-heterogeneity diagnostics: correlations with moderators, BMA with and without SE, and estimator-split funnels, especially GMM versus non-GMM.
3. Rebuild Table 6 with common-support or leverage checks and feasible high-precision benchmarks alongside SE = 0.
4. State a decision rule for the finance range and show the full correction-method range in the abstract or an adjacent sensitivity sentence.
5. Make the calibration-estimate wedge descriptive unless the samples are made comparable or the calibration sample is also bias-adjusted.
6. Add robustness for field classification, plug-in choices, winsorization cutoffs, and study-level weighting.
7. Verify reference-list completeness and table-note clarity before resubmission.
