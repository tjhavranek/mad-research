# MAD-research final memo (Claude-only arm)

## Verdict
This is a careful, transparent, and methodologically current meta-analysis of relative risk aversion (RRA) estimated from the consumption Euler equation, and on the most demanding axes — identification credibility, statistical machinery, and robustness coverage — it is genuinely strong: the funnel-asymmetry logic is stated with its identifying assumption named and then relaxed across nine specifications (four linear, five non-linear), and model uncertainty in the moderator analysis is handled by Bayesian model averaging with explicit prior-robustness. The faults the three audits surface, after I verified every load-bearing quote against the PDF, concentrate almost entirely in the *presentation of the headline numbers* rather than in the analysis that produced them: the finance corrected mean is reported with three mutually inconsistent ranges across the paper ("2–7", "2–6", and "1–3"), the abstract's economics "1" travels without the wide interval the paper's own preferred context object attaches to it, and the "best practice" calibration recommendation rests on an undocumented, un-sensitivity-tested plug-in vector. None of these is fatal and none overturns the qualitative conclusions (large publication bias; economics ≈ 1; finance higher; stockholders less risk-averse; strong context-dependence). The single most consequential identification-side gap that survives is upstream of every correction: the meta-analysis corrects for selective *reporting* but takes the *internal validity* of each primary Euler-equation estimate as given, so primary-level weak-instrument or misspecification bias would survive into the intercept undetected. The correct disposition is acceptance conditional on reconciling the finance ranges, attaching uncertainty to the scalar headlines, and demoting the calibration recommendation to an explicitly assumption-indexed range.

## Top surviving criticisms (by severity)

### 1. The finance corrected mean is reported with three mutually inconsistent ranges, and the abstract's upper bound is not produced by any correction technique
- Severity: High
- Evidence: Abstract: "a mean risk aversion of 1 in economics and 2–7 in finance contexts" (p.2315), echoed at "The corrected mean relative risk aversion is 1 in the economics literature and 2–7 in the finance literature" (p.2317) and "The corrected mean estimate is 1 for economics and 2–7 for finance" (p.2328). But the body says "relative risk aversion around 1 in economics and 2–6 in finance" (p.2322), and a *third* range appears: "The corrected mean coefficient of relative risk aversion is around 1 for economics and 1–3 for finance, compared with uncorrected means of 7.5 and 45" (p.2320). The five Table 3 Panel C finance corrections are 2.535, 11.196, 6.100, 2.390, 0.625 (p.2322); the non-outlier core is 2.39–6.10, and no technique yields ≈7. The "7" is silently imported from the Table 6 "Finance" implied value of 7.31 (p.2327), which the paper itself confirms: "The implied estimate for finance is somewhat larger, around 7, but not far from the 2–6 range" (p.2328).
- Why it matters: The single most-cited deliverable of the paper carries three different upper bounds (3, 6, 7) depending on the page, and the abstract's headline number is not generated by the correction machinery it is presented as summarizing — it is a plug-in-conditioned BMA fitted value from a different exercise. Downstream calibrators will quote a number the correction table does not support. The audits caught "2–7 vs 2–6"; the "1–3" on p.2320 (which all three audits missed) makes the inconsistency materially worse.

### 2. The economics "1" and the finance scalars are reported with a precision the paper's own intervals and method-dispersion contradict
- Severity: High
- Evidence: Abstract states "a mean risk aversion of 1 in economics" (p.2315), but the paper's own context synthesis reports "Economics 1.10 [−2.11, 4.30]" and "Finance 7.31 [4.28, 10.33]" (Table 6, p.2327), with the text conceding "such an exercise yields quite imprecise results" (p.2327). The selection model gives finance 11.196 (Table 3 Panel C, p.2322), flagged in text as "The selection model yields a large estimate for finance, 11" (p.2321).
- Why it matters: The economics 95% interval spans negative (risk-loving) through risk-neutral to 4.30, so the scalar "1" is statistically indistinguishable from risk-neutrality under the paper's own preferred best-practice object. Even the tighter core WLS economics constant, 1.085 with CI [0.654, 2.059] (Table 2 Panel B, p.2321), admits values double the point estimate. Reporting unqualified scalars in the abstract conveys far more certainty than the tables support and strips the uncertainty the authors acknowledge internally.

### 3. The "best practice" calibration recommendation rests on a subjective, un-sensitivity-tested plug-in vector yet is promoted to policy
- Severity: Medium-High
- Evidence: "This approach is inevitably subjective but we still believe it adds value to meta-analysis... We plug zero for the standard error... we plug in sample maxima for the time span and midpoint... we plug in 0.5 for Epstein–Zin... All other variables... are set to their sample means" (p.2327). Table 6 reports "Overall best practice 3.74 [0.99, 6.50]", "Economics 1.10", "Finance 7.31" (p.2327). These are explicitly elevated to policy: "the values we recommend for calibration" (p.2317, repeated p.2328).
- Why it matters: A recommendation marketed for downstream calibration carries an implied robustness that a single researcher-chosen point in a ~30-dimensional plug-in grid does not have. Defensible alternative choices (GMM is 59% prevalent per Table 4, p.2324, so arguably "best practice"; or Epstein–Zin = 1 rather than 0.5) would move the numbers materially, yet the reported CIs capture only sampling uncertainty holding the plug-ins fixed — specification uncertainty over the plug-in vector itself is nowhere reflected. Both the estimand lane and the overclaim lane independently flagged this; the convergence strengthens it.

### 4. The headline pooled means blend a documented 10% non-Epstein–Zin subgroup whose conditional offset is ~5 units, with no Epstein–Zin-only headline reported
- Severity: Medium
- Evidence: Table 4 defines "Epstein–Zin = 1 if preferences are of the Epstein–Zin type (the remaining estimates are derived from specifications with internal habits, expected utility with a reference level of consumption, ambiguity aversion, or disappointment aversion) ... Mean 0.90" (p.2324). The Epstein–Zin moderator carries PIP 0.991 with posterior mean 5.488 (Table 5, p.2326).
- Why it matters: The abstract restricts the estimand to studies that "separate risk aversion from intertemporal substitution" (p.2315), but the pooled Table 1 / abstract means are computed over a sample that is 10% non-EZ (habits, ambiguity, disappointment aversion). The paper's own strongest moderator shows the estimate shifts by ~5 units with preference type, so a single pooled headline obscures a large, verifiable within-sample heterogeneity in the target object. This sits at the estimand boundary, but the reproducibility core — a documented 10% subgroup with a ~5-unit offset never broken out — is concrete. (Mitigated, not dissolved, by the fact that EZ is controlled inside BMA and Table 6.)

### 5. Three of the eight PIP > 0.5 moderators are not significant at 5% in the paper's own frequentist check, but the text reports this only as "borderline"
- Severity: Medium
- Evidence: The text states there are "eight variables with posterior inclusion probability above 0.5, which means that these variables are systematically related to the published coefficients" and that the OLS check "finds borderline statistical significance for several of the variables" (p.2323). But Table 5 (p.2326) shows the frequentist p-values for Epstein–Zin (0.099), Stockholder (0.115), and Nonseparable durables (0.135) all exceed 0.09, while the data dummies clear 5% (Quarterly 0.009, US 0.000, Finance journal 0.000, Cross-section 0.026).
- Why it matters: The conclusion lists "general form of the utility function (Epstein–Zin vs. other approaches)" and "whether or not the researcher focuses on stockholders" as established context drivers (p.2328), yet the paper's own non-Bayesian cross-check does not reject the null for either at 5%. The insignificant variables cluster precisely on the *substantive* (utility-form, stockholder) moderators rather than the data-mechanical dummies; "borderline" flattens a moderator-specific weakness in exactly the claims the conclusion foregrounds. This is the cleanest in-paper internal-consistency item neither the estimand nor the overclaim lane surfaced, and it is verifiable to the digit.

### 6. The corrected mean is read off the SE = 0 intercept under a linearity-of-selection assumption the paper itself calls "strong" and "low-power," and the five relaxations disagree by an order of magnitude in finance without an adjudicating diagnostic
- Severity: Medium
- Evidence: "the intercept can be interpreted as the mean coefficient of relative risk aversion corrected for publication bias: If we assume that publication bias is indeed a linear function of the standard error. (This is a strong assumption that we will later relax...)" (p.2320); "this test for publication bias is known to have relatively low power (Stanley 2008)" (p.2320). The finance relaxations span 0.625 (van Aert–van Assen) to 11.196 (Andrews–Kasy) (Table 3 Panel C, p.2322), resolved verbally: "The selection model yields a large estimate for finance, 11. The remaining results are more consistent" (pp.2321–2323). The SE has "Mean 76.65, SD 730.63" (Table 4, p.2324).
- Why it matters: The finance estimand is identified only up to the selection mechanism, and when five estimators of that mechanism produce values from 0.6 to 11, the data are not pinning down the mechanism — the analyst is, by narrative exclusion of the two extremes. The paper notes specification tests of the Andrews–Kasy model exist in Supplement C2/C3 (p.2323) but does not bring them to bear on choosing the finance estimand. The right presentation is an assumption-indexed range (0.6–11, each endpoint tagged to its model), not a verbally-pruned "2–7". (Severity held at Medium because the disclosure is candid and the economics intercept is corroborated by the non-extrapolation stem and selection methods.)

## Minority report
One grounded objection should survive panel consensus even if the majority treats these as cosmetic: the Table 6 numbers labeled "the values we recommend for calibration" (p.2317, p.2328) are the paper's single genuinely prescriptive output, and recommending field-specific RRA values (finance 7.31, economics 1.10) for downstream calibration on the basis of one undocumented plug-in vector — when the authors themselves call the exercise "quite imprecise" and "inevitably subjective" (p.2327) and the economics interval [−2.11, 4.30] crosses zero — is a normative claim that materially exceeds the identified evidence. It should be downgraded from a "recommendation" to an "illustration," regardless of how well the upstream correction machinery performs. This is not merely an imprecision quibble: a recommendation invites mechanical adoption ("just quote 1"), which is exactly the Mehra–Prescott "just quote 10" behavior the paper criticizes (p.2318), now risked in reverse.

## Conventional-referee assumption check
A conventional referee would likely (i) accept the "first meta-analysis" priority claim at face value, (ii) treat the nine-specification + BMA robustness program as dispositive of publication-bias concerns, and (iii) quote "1 in economics, 2–7 in finance" as the paper's settled result. This audit qualifies all three. The priority claim is narrowly true but the *substantive* finding is less novel than framed: Havranek (2015)'s EIS meta-analysis already implied RRA ≈ 3 via the reciprocal ("the finding translates to the relative risk aversion of 3", p.2316), and the paper then recommends 3 for finance ("power utility with relative risk aversion set at 3 thus seems relatively consistent", p.2317) without reconciling the coincidence — so the EZ route is positioned as superseding the reciprocal route while reproducing its headline number. The robustness program, though broad, varies *estimators and priors* but not the *data-construction* choices that most move the heavy-tailed finance mean: the 5% winsorization (Table 1 note, p.2318) and the multiple-estimates-per-study weighting are applied but never sensitivity-tested against unwinsorized/alternative-trim versions, even though the economics/finance contrast hinges on the right tail (finance mean 45.05 vs median 17.82, Table 1, p.2318). And the headline scalars, as criticisms #1–#2 show, are internally inconsistent and uncertainty-stripped. The deepest assumption a conventional referee would not question — and should — is that each collected primary estimate is internally valid; the paper's own citation to Ascari et al. (2021) on weak instruments and a "wide" RRA range (p.2316) implies primary-level bias that every correction here would pass through into the intercept untouched.

## Action list
1. Reconcile the finance corrected mean to a single, sourced statement. Pick one range and use it everywhere, or explicitly label which number comes from the Table 3 corrections versus the Table 6 plug-in exercise. The "1–3" (p.2320), "2–6" (p.2322), and "2–7" (abstract/p.2317/p.2328) must not coexist unflagged.
2. Attach Table 6's intervals to the abstract and conclusion scalars. Report economics as "≈1 but imprecise [−2.11, 4.30]" and finance as an assumption-indexed range (≈0.6–11 across selection models, core 2–6), so the uncertainty travels with the number.
3. Demote "the values we recommend for calibration" (p.2317, p.2328) to "illustrative," and add a tornado/grid sensitivity over the plug-in vector (vary Epstein–Zin = 0.5 vs 1; GMM vs OLS as best practice) so specification uncertainty enters Table 6's intervals.
4. Report an Epstein–Zin-only headline mean alongside the pooled mean, given the 10% non-EZ mass (Table 4, p.2324) and the ~5-unit EZ offset (Table 5, p.2326).
5. State explicitly which PIP > 0.5 moderators clear 5% in the OLS check and which do not; soften the conclusion's claims for Epstein–Zin (p=0.099), Stockholder (p=0.115), and Nonseparable durables (p=0.135) from "systematically related" to "robust under BMA but borderline frequentist."
6. Bring the existing Andrews–Kasy specification tests (Supplement C2/C3, p.2323) into the main text for the finance subsample to adjudicate the order-of-magnitude disagreement among selection models, rather than resolving it by narrative.
7. Add a winsorization/trimming robustness check (unwinsorized and alternative-trim corrected means) to demonstrate the finance headline is not an artifact of the 5% winsorization (p.2318).
8. Add a primary-validity moderator or diagnostic (e.g., weak-IV-robust subsample) to separate well-identified from weakly-identified primary estimates, addressing the biggest blind spot: the corrections handle selective reporting but not primary-level internal validity.
9. Reconcile the new finance value (3) with Havranek (2015)'s reciprocal-implied 3 (p.2316), stating whether the agreement corroborates or coincides, and soften "first meta-analysis" to "first meta-analysis of directly estimated RRA coefficients that separate risk aversion from EIS."
