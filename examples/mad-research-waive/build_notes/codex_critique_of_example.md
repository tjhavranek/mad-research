# Codex critique of the WAIVE MAD-research example

The example is substantively useful, but not clean enough to ship publicly without revision. It demonstrates that the protocol can produce real, uncomfortable criticism of friendly work. It also demonstrates several protocol leaks that non-programmer users would not be able to diagnose.

On grounding, the final memo's top-five criticisms mostly cite real source material. The slide 20 and 21 quotes match `manuscript_text.md`, and the MAIVE p. 4 and p. 7 quotes are present. I do not see a fabricated locator in the main top-five list. The problem is subtler: synthesis lets unsupported reasoning enter as if it were grounded. The clearest breach is in "Points rejected": the F-threshold row says MAIVE "already recommends Anderson-Rubin CIs and F>100 as defensive practice." F>100 is in the source; Anderson-Rubin CIs are not in `manuscript_text.md`. Likewise, "External considerations: None used in any stream" is too clean. The Methodologist used PDF-only material and package/inference claims that the Codex streams could not verify from the audit source. If the public claim is source-grounded auditing, the final memo should label those as outside the shared text corpus.

The "Points rejected" section is partly honest but incomplete. Rejecting the EasyMeta deployment objection is fair: slide 17 says the software supports WAIVE; it does not by itself claim validation. Rejecting the preregistration criticism is also fair under grounding rules. Merging Evidence Auditor R1 #5 into surviving criticism #5 is reasonable. But synthesis dropped at least two valid points that should have survived in some form. Evidence Auditor R1 #4, on reproducibility defaults, is grounded in slide 12's loose language about controls/logs and slide 17's multilevel/clustering claim. More importantly, Contribution Skeptic R2's "double-counting" objection is strong: MAIVE drops the first-stage residual from adjusted SEs, then WAIVE reuses negative residuals to downweight observations. That is a coherent estimand-level criticism and should not disappear.

The minority report is mislabelled. The final memo presents the negative-residual interpretive jump as a minority view, but it is the majority consensus: all three streams ranked it high, and synthesis made it surviving criticism #1. The actual minority candidate was the Contribution Skeptic R2 double-counting objection, explicitly designated as minority in that file. Calling a consensus point a "minority report" makes the protocol look performative.

Role isolation mostly held procedurally in Round 2: the Codex streams appear to have seen only peer audits, not their own Round 1 outputs. But lane discipline blurred. The Evidence Auditor made contribution-claim arguments, and the Contribution Skeptic raised a technical first-stage residual critique. That is not fatal, but the example should not oversell the roles as cleanly separated expert lanes.

The final memo also overclaims independence and certainty. "Three independent streams" is too strong when two streams are the same Codex model reading the same extraction, and the synthesizer orchestrated the process. The ablation criticism is valid, but it is partly an extraction-limit criticism because slides 22-26 were truncated and explicitly described as results/applications/conclusions. The memo acknowledges this caveat late, but the verdict should carry the caveat earlier. Conversely, the memo underclaims implementation reproducibility and generated-residual uncertainty, both important for a method users might try to implement.

For a public non-programmer demo, this example is compelling but risky. It shows the skill producing concrete, useful objections. It also contains mojibake, missing source coverage, an unsupported synthesis claim, and a fake-looking minority report. Non-programmers may conclude the protocol enforces grounding and adjudication more reliably than it did here. I would ship it only after fixing the final memo: remove unsupported Anderson-Rubin language, make the extraction caveat prominent, restore the double-counting/reproducibility objections, and relabel or delete the minority report.
